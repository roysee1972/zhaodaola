LEVEL 07 · MONEY & WEALTH

Yu Yunwen

—Paying Others' Poll Tax

Life Principle
Don't just stare at the problem itself — look for alternative solutions and wasted resources already present in the system.

⏳ The Setting

During the Southern Song Dynasty, commoners in the Zhe region were required to pay an annual poll tax in the form of silk cloth. The burden was so crushing that impoverished families abandoned newborn babies or killed young children to avoid the tax. The population was in catastrophic decline, and society's moral fabric was unraveling.

⚖ The Dilemma

The poll tax system was driving families to commit infanticide — abandoning newborns and killing infants rather than registering them. Population was plummeting, and social morality was collapsing. But the court needed the revenue from the poll tax, and abolishing it outright was politically impossible.

💡 The Strategy

Yu Yunwen reframed the question. Instead of asking 'Should we abolish the poll tax?' he asked 'Is there another source of revenue that can replace it?' Through investigation, he discovered that reed fields along the river were highly profitable — but they had been illegally seized by powerful aristocratic families and wealthy temples. Yu Yunwen ordered a thorough inventory of these reed fields, then petitioned the court to use their income to replace the people's poll tax. The powerful families lost their illicit monopoly; the common people were freed from their crushing burden.

🏛 The Outcome

The people were freed from the poll tax, and the practice of abandoning and killing infants ceased. Families experienced, for the first time, the simple joy of raising their children together. The population began to recover, and public resources that had been captured by the powerful were returned to their rightful purpose.

💛 Why This Matters

The phrase 'for the first time, people knew the joy of father and son living together' is one of the most moving lines in this entire collection. Behind the policy argument lies a deeply human story: parents forced to choose between feeding the children they already had and registering the ones just born. Yu Yunwen's wisdom didn't just balance a budget — it restored the most fundamental human bond. This reminds us that the true measure of any economic policy is not the numbers on a ledger, but the lives it touches.

🔮 The Deeper Lesson

The deeper lesson is about creative problem-solving in governance: when facing a seemingly impossible trade-off (the people need relief, but the treasury needs revenue), don't just argue about the trade-off — look for hidden resources in the system. The reed fields had always been there, generating profits that were being siphoned off by the powerful. Yu Yunwen's genius was in recognizing that the solution wasn't to tax more or spend less, but to redirect existing wealth that was being wasted. This principle applies broadly: in organizations, families, and communities, the resources needed to solve problems often already exist — they're just being captured by those who shouldn't have them.

Reflection

1 Yu Yunwen didn't just argue against the tax — he found an alternative revenue source. When facing a problem at work or in life, do you typically argue against the status quo, or do you propose viable alternatives?
2 Powerful families had monopolized public resources for their own benefit. Can you think of modern examples where public resources are captured by private interests? What would it take to reclaim them?
3 The phrase 'for the first time, people knew the joy of father and son together' is deeply moving. How do economic policies affect family bonds and human relationships in your own experience?
