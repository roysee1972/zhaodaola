LEVEL 02 · LEARNING & EDUCATION

Li Su (李愬)

Honor the Court, Establish the Order of Authority

Life Principle
Authority is not self-proclaimed — it must be demonstrated through ritual and symbolism. The most powerful leaders are those who willingly subordinate themselves to a higher principle, because that act of deference elevates everyone who witnesses it.

⏳ The Setting

During the Yuanhe reign of Emperor Xianzong of the Tang Dynasty, the warlord Wu Yuanji had maintained a separatist regime in the Huaixi region for years. The court dispatched General Li Su (李愬) to suppress the rebellion. After a legendary night raid, Li Su captured the rebel stronghold of Caizhou. But military victory alone was not enough — the people of Caizhou had lived under rebel rule for decades and had lost all sense of the court's authority. A new kind of battle needed to be fought: one for hearts and minds.

⚖ The Dilemma

Li Su faced a subtle but critical problem. He could hold Caizhou by force, but force alone would never make the local population genuinely accept the court's rule. Without a shift in their mental framework — their understanding of who held legitimate authority — the region could rebel again at any moment. How do you teach an entire population about a hierarchy they have never personally witnessed?

💡 The Strategy

Li Su devised a brilliant piece of political theater. When Pei Du (裴度), the court's chief representative and the emperor's personal envoy, arrived to inspect the newly captured city, Li Su met him fully armed and in full military regalia — then knelt on the left side of the road in formal salute. Pei Du, startled, tried to step aside and avoid the gesture. But Li Su made a quiet, extraordinary request: "The people of Cai have been stubborn and rebellious; they have not understood the proper order of authority for decades. I beg you, sir, to allow this display — so that they may see it." Li Su was not kneeling for his own benefit. He was performing for an audience of thousands — showing them that even the hero who conquered the city bowed before the court's representative.

🏛 The Outcome

The ceremony achieved what military force alone could not. The people of Caizhou witnessed, with their own eyes, that the general who had conquered their city — a man of immense military accomplishment — still submitted to the authority of the emperor's envoy. This single symbolic act reestablished the mental framework of imperial authority in their minds. It was a lesson delivered not through lectures or threats, but through living theater.

💛 Why This Matters

There is something profoundly moving about a victorious general choosing humility over glory. Li Su had every reason to bask in his triumph — he had achieved what entire armies had failed to do for years. Instead, he used his moment of triumph to elevate someone else, to demonstrate a principle larger than himself. In a world where leaders often claim all credit and hoard all prestige, Li Su's gesture reminds us that the greatest authority comes from those who willingly share it.

🔮 The Deeper Lesson

The underlying logic is that authority requires visible rituals to sustain itself. Abstract concepts like "the court's power" mean nothing to people who have never seen them embodied in concrete actions. Li Su understood that authority is not a fact — it is a performance, repeated until it becomes belief. In modern management, this principle endures: leaders who model respect for organizational values — who visibly follow the rules they preach — build cultures of trust far more effectively than those who rely on position alone.

Reflection

1 Think of a leader you admire. Do they demonstrate their authority through symbolic acts of deference to something larger than themselves — or do they rely solely on their position? How does this affect your trust in them?
2 In your own organization, what rituals or symbolic acts reinforce the culture and values? Could you design a "Li Su moment" — a visible demonstration that teaches people what matters?
3 Li Su chose to kneel even though he was the hero. Have you ever voluntarily subordinated yourself to a principle or institution, even when you had the power not to? What was the result?
