LEVEL 09 · INTERESTS & PASSIONS

李广王越

—虚张声势与隐秘撤退

Life Principle
In desperate straits, do not let fear dominate you — calmly analyze the optimal strategy. Sometimes "not playing by the rules" is actually the safest choice. Retreat is not shameful; a planned withdrawal is a supreme tactical art. Leadership is demonstrated through composure and correct decisions at critical moments.

⏳ The Setting

During the Western Han dynasty, Lǐ Guǎng (李广) led just over a hundred cavalry on a border patrol when they encountered several thousand Xiongnu riders — a staggering disparity in numbers. In the Ming dynasty, Wáng Yuè (王越) and the Duke of Bǎoguó, Zhū Yǒng (朱永), led a thousand men on a border patrol and similarly came under sudden attack by enemy cavalry. Two cases separated by a millennium, both facing the desperate situation of "enemies vastly outnumbering us, fleeing means certain death" — yet both commanders turned the tide through composure under fire.

⚖ The Dilemma

Lǐ Guǎng faced several thousand Xiongnu with just over a hundred riders. If he turned to flee, the Xiongnu would shoot them down and annihilate the entire force. If he stayed to confront them, the numerical gap was so enormous they could be wiped out at any moment. Wáng Yuè's thousand men were ambushed by enemy cavalry — the enemy was fresh while his own troops were exhausted from a long march. Duke Zhū Yǒng's instinct was to retreat.

🎯 Motives

Lǐ Guǎng's core judgment was "to flee is to die, to stay is to live" — running meant certain death, while staying might actually save them. His goal was to make the Xiongnu believe he was bait for a larger army hidden behind him. Wáng Yuè's goal was to stabilize his position, then find an opportunity to slip away — first forming up to deter the enemy from advancing rashly, then quietly withdrawing under cover of darkness.

🧩 The Framework

Lǐ Guǎng employed "reverse reasoning": normally, a hundred riders meeting several thousand would flee — so not fleeing actually implied an ambush. He turned his own fear into the basis for his judgment: if I were a Xiongnu warrior and saw a hundred riders who didn't run, what would I think? Wáng Yuè used "blending strength and weakness": first displaying strength through formation to make the enemy hesitate, then withdrawing under cover of night with bits in the horses' mouths.

🧠 The Mindset

Lǐ Guǎng showed no fear in mortal danger, maintaining calm while his hundred riders were terrified, making the counter-intuitive decision to "advance instead of retreat." When he ordered "dismount and unsaddle," his subordinates must have been horrified, but Lǐ Guǎng's composure infected the entire force. Wáng Yuè decisively stopped Duke Zhū Yǒng when he wanted to flee, embodying the courage of one whose "expression does not change even if Mount Tai collapses before him."

💡 The Strategy

Lǐ Guǎng's strategy unfolded in three steps: first, advance to within two li of the Xiongnu and stop, displaying fearlessness; second, dismount and unsaddle the horses, signaling no intention to flee; third, shoot and kill a white-horse general to demonstrate martial prowess, then return to the unit and unsaddle the horses while letting them graze freely, creating the illusion of "waiting for the main army." Wáng Yuè's strategy: first form a defensive line to make the enemy hesitate; at dusk, dismount and march in single file with bits in the horses' mouths; Wáng Yuè personally led the bravest men as rearguard, slipping away quietly from behind the mountains.

🔬 The Mental Model

The core model is "counter-intuitive operation": when everyone thinks you should flee, you stay; when the enemy expects you to resist, you withdraw. Both Lǐ Guǎng and Wáng Yuè found answers that defied instinct between "normal reaction" and "optimal strategy." The key lay in reading the enemy's psychology — the enemy would infer your intentions from your behavior.

🏛 The Outcome

The Xiongnu spent the whole night in paranoia and withdrew at midnight. Lǐ Guǎng's hundred riders emerged unscathed. Wáng Yuè's army marched fifty li through the night with bits in their mouths and reached the city walls, completely undetected by the enemy cavalry. Both engagements were classics of "subduing the enemy without fighting" — not a single sword was crossed; psychological warfare alone defused mortal peril.

💛 Why This Matters

Lǐ Guǎng's composure when all hundred riders were terrified demonstrated the extraordinary courage of a great general. His ease when ordering "dismount and unsaddle" was a transcendence of life and death. Wáng Yuè's decisive intervention when the Duke wanted to flee displayed true leadership — making the right call at the critical moment, even when that call defied intuition.

🔮 The Deeper Lesson

The essence of "bluffing" is exploiting information asymmetry: the enemy does not know your true strength, and your behavior is their only source of intelligence. Extended to business competition, a small company's "confident posture" when facing industry giants is itself a form of deterrence. "Covert withdrawal" is the wisdom of preserving strength when the situation is unfavorable — staying alive matters more than saving face.
