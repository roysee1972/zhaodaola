Game of Life — Family of Origin:
Happiness and Impermanence

Zhaodaola · The Twelve Levels of the Game of Life

Life is short, and life is also long. If I had to find what makes an ordinary life worth living, I think it would be this: those fleeting yet precious, impermanent yet enduring moments of happiness.

Let me share a few such moments from my own ordinary life.

Moment One: Two people, singing freely on the sidewalk

My wife and I once had a truly wonderful time as a couple. Back then, neither of us earned much. Every month, we had to carefully budget our income, and my wife kept a dedicated ledger. If we overspent, she'd look worried and announce we needed to tighten our belts. If there was money left over, she'd beam with joy and drag me out to a restaurant.

A classmate of mine — he was working alone in a distant city at the time, and his wife was also our classmate, all of us good friends — once told me he loved calling her at night, and would even sing to her over the phone: "The midnight in a foreign city is so cold and quiet, a man and an eager heart…"

His words moved me for a long time. My wife and I had our own stretch of sidewalk serenading too. We lived near the Third Ring Road back then, with relentless traffic and noise, yet the two of us stood on the curb belting out songs at the top of our lungs — she sang "The Qinghai-Tibet Plateau" and I sang "O Sole Mio" — lost in the joy of it, not noticing time passing.

Off-key, out of tune, pedestrians staring — we didn't care one bit.

The noisy streets became our stage,
the dim streetlights our spotlight.

We had candlelit dinners too — not at a fancy restaurant, but at home. A single birthday candle, two simple dishes. We went on outings as well, packing a water bottle and some bread, hopping on our bicycles and riding off to somewhere we'd never been. The scenery didn't matter. Money didn't matter.

Those brief moments of happiness seemed ordinary at the time. Looking back, every single one is worth treasuring.

Moment Two: After our daughter was born — the family's "golden hours"

After our daughter was born, our family entered what I'd call a period of "high-concentration happiness." Everyone orbited around her. She was innocent, lively, and loved to play — we were all her playmates. My wife and I took turns building blocks with her, pretending to be animals, rolling around on the floor. My in-laws came too — two normally serious, reserved elders who, in the presence of their granddaughter, transformed into "old children" — crawling on all fours as her horse, hiding behind curtains for peekaboo, laughing louder than the child herself.

Those days when the entire family treated one thing — "playing with the child" — as the top priority, I still think of them as a luxury even now.

Because they required conditions: everyone needed to be healthy, relationships needed to be harmonious, and nobody could be crushed by the weight of making a living. Having all three conditions met at the same time is not easy.

Peaceful days, a book for company — the quiet contentment of ordinary life

Our living room had a huge mirror, and my daughter and I often danced in front of it — dances we made up ourselves. Sometimes we imitated robots from TV, sometimes we tried street dance moves, sometimes we mimicked the square dancing of elderly folks. In short, two clowns frequently appeared before that mirror, laughter never ceasing. But ten years later, we moved. That big mirror gathered dust. My daughter, now a teenager, didn't even want to talk to me.

Those beautiful moments gradually faded into the dust of time.

Moment Three: The elders' example — happiness forged from hardship

This one feels both extravagant and easily overlooked.

My father, in his seventies, went back to our hometown alone and spent four months renovating an entire house by himself. The place turned out beautifully, but to help take care of their granddaughter, my parents made the bold decision to move to Chengdu. The new house only got used a few days a year, during Spring Festival.

Now my father is over eighty, and still going strong. A while back, he needed dental implants, which meant repeated trips to a hospital in the city center — two bus transfers each way, over two hours one way. He didn't ask anyone to go with him. He looked up the route himself, left in the morning, came back in the afternoon, and got it done.

We asked: Want to take a taxi? Want us to drive you?

He said: "It's just a small thing. Don't worry about it."

Eighty-something years old, alone, two hours by bus, registering, waiting, treatment, surgery, picking up medication, then two hours back. What does that mean? It means his body still works. It means his mind is still sharp. It means he still has the confidence to handle things on his own.

My mother is nearly eighty too, in much worse health than my father — severe osteoporosis, nicknamed "porcelain doll." In recent years, she's had three or four fractures. But her attitude is wonderful; she's a devout Buddhist. At every family meal, she's a determined doggy-bag packer. Even if there's just one spare rib and a small bite of rice left, she won't leave them behind — she packs everything up. Sometimes the leftovers sit in the fridge too long and go bad, and she'll say: "That's okay — give it to the bugs and ants."

My father-in-law farms vegetables in the district next to Longquan. Every year, he harvests hundreds of jin of rapeseed. He's been at it for over a decade, calling himself "an old farmer." He never leaves leftovers unfinished. Gnawed bones don't get thrown away either — he says: "Save them for Xiaohua." Xiaohua is a stray dog that hangs around his vegetable patch.

"Enough to eat is enough. Worrying more won't help." That sounds plain, but in today's anxiety-ridden world, it's practically a luxury-grade sedative.

What are the true highlights of an ordinary life?

If we treat "life's luxuries" as a serious topic, they're probably spread across several dimensions —

🌿
Physical Dimension: A Sense of Energy

It's not about marathon times — it's about the baseline of "waking up without aches and pains, having enough energy to get through the day." Many people start overdrawing in their twenties: staying up late, sitting all day, chaotic eating, constant emotional stress. By the time the body flashes red, you realize all those days of "it's fine, I can handle it" were actually the most expensive ones.

Good health is the foundation of every "luxury." When the foundation crumbles, nothing else can stand.

☀️
Inner Dimension: Building Your Own Happiness System

Everyone has a "happiness system" inside — the small things that make you feel "life is good." For some, it's reading a great book. For others, brewing a pot of fine tea. For others still, listening to the wind during an evening walk.

The problem is, for many people, this system gradually goes offline with age. Work gets busier, children demand more attention, parents start aging, your own energy declines — and if your happiness system has been dormant for a long time, you drift into "mechanical mode": doing things every day, but rarely feeling.

Rebuilding the happiness system doesn't mean vacations or shopping. It means allowing yourself to be moved by small things again — a bowl of noodles that tastes incredible, and you pause for a moment. Flowers blooming wildly by the roadside, and you stop for five seconds. Your child says something funny, and you laugh — not a polite laugh, but a genuine one.

⚖️
Material and Energy: Enough, vs. Never Enough

The role of material things is to provide a "floor of security," not to fill "the bottomless pit of desire." The truly wise approach is to find your own boundary between "enough" and "too much": beyond that line, more possessions won't bring more happiness — only more entanglement and anxiety.

My mother's "enough to eat is enough" isn't negativity — it's clarity she earned by living to eighty. Many people spend their entire lives never crossing the mental threshold of "material wealth = happiness."

Happiness is something passed down

Why can I sing loudly on the sidewalk without caring what strangers think? Why do I consider a single candle and two simple dishes a proper candlelit dinner, rather than settling for less? Why do I see clowning around with my daughter in front of a mirror as one of life's great pleasures, rather than a waste of time?

Probably because that's how I was raised in my family of origin.

My father's "it's just a small thing — don't worry about it" is a quiet confidence in his own capability. My mother's "enough to eat is enough" is a clarity about life. My father-in-law's bones saved for the stray dog are a habit of gentleness toward the world.

These aren't grand philosophies. They're ways of living. A judgment system about "what's worth caring about and what isn't" — etched into your bones, day after day, without you even noticing.

We often say that family of origin leaves scars. That's true. But what our families of origin leave us isn't just scars — there's also nourishment. And often, we only see the scars and forget the nourishment. Today, I'm trying to remember the nourishment.

Happiness was never expensive — we made it expensive

Today's young people face a narrative of happiness far more intense than our generation's. Social media slaps the standard for "the good life" right in your face — you need taste, you need pour-over coffee, you need a recognizable bag, you need Instagram-worthy posts. Quality of life must "only go up," and once you've gotten used to a certain spending level, you can never accept "an ordinary person's life" again.

It's a one-way track of desire: you're forever chasing a higher standard, and you'll never reach the end.

The deeper problem is equating "purchasing power" with "life's worth." If someone can only define themselves by "what bag I carry, where I travel," their sense of self is extremely fragile — because those things can be taken away at any time.

True self should be built on things that can't be taken away: your physical condition, your relationships with family, and your ability to find joy in ordinary days.

In the midst of impermanence, finding what's worth cherishing

All happiness is impermanent. The two clowns in front of the mirror will eventually part ways. The sidewalk singing will eventually stop. The elders' quiet certainty will eventually reach its end too.

It is precisely because of impermanence that those moments are precious. Not because they were perfect, but because they truly existed — and because we were truly present for them.

Psychologists say that happiness comes from two elements: first, that something wonderful happens; second, that you notice it. The second part is actually harder than the first.

Now, I often do this: at an ordinary dinner table, or on a walk, or while watching an old movie with my wife — I pause, and silently say to myself:

This moment is pretty good.
Just that. Nothing more needed.

This is the philosophy of happiness I learned from my family of origin —

Simple, understated, but with weight.
