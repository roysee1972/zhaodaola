The Ji'an Clerk

—Prostitute Catches Thief

Life Principle
Do not be deceived by surface information; verify the source and authenticity of information. In solving cases, protecting the dignity of those involved is as important as pursuing the truth. 'Knowing information does not equal knowing a person' is an important principle for exposing lies.

⏳ The Setting

During the Ming Dynasty, a peculiar case occurred in Ji'an Prefecture: a thief sneaked into a wealthy family's bridal chamber on their wedding night, hiding under the bed to steal when the opportunity arose, but was discovered due to hunger. After being captured, the thief boldly claimed to be a doctor, saying he had been summoned to treat the bride's illness. Since the thief could describe intimate details about the bride (words exchanged between the pillows), the magistrate nearly believed his story.

⚖ The Dilemma

The thief's lie was very clever - he claimed to be a doctor brought in to treat the bride's illness, and could describe intimate details about her. Since he had indeed been hiding under the bed and overheard private conversations between the pillows, this information was genuine. The magistrate faced a dilemma: how could he prove the thief was lying without humiliating the bride?

🎯 Motives

The veteran clerk's goal was to expose the thief's lie while protecting the bride's dignity. He proposed a clever method: using a courtesan to stand in for the bride during confrontation. The thief's motive was to escape punishment; he exploited the 'doctor' identity and the bride's privacy to defend himself.

🧩 The Framework

The veteran clerk adopted a 'identity verification plus privacy protection' cognitive framework. He recognized that although the thief knew the bride's intimate information, he did not know the bride personally. Therefore, substituting a courtesan for the bride in confrontation would reveal the thief's lie if he failed to recognize the courtesan. This approach also protected the bride's privacy and dignity.

🧠 The Mindset

The veteran clerk was shrewd and compassionate - he wanted to solve the case while also protecting the bride's dignity. He pointed out that 'the bride has just married, and regardless of whether she wins or loses the case, the humiliation would be immeasurable.' This concern for human nature demonstrated both his wisdom and compassion.

💡 The Strategy

The veteran clerk's strategy had three steps: first, pointing out that although the thief knew the bride's intimate details, he did not know the bride personally ('a thief who sneaks in and out would certainly not recognize the woman'); second, using a courtesan to stand in for the bride during confrontation ('substituting another woman'); third, the thief did not recognize the courtesan, yet exclaimed 'You invited me to treat an illness, and now you arrest me as a thief?' - this exposed his lie.

🔬 The Mental Model

The core model is 'identity verification' - although the thief knew the bride's intimate information, he did not know the bride personally. By substituting a courtesan for the bride in confrontation, the thief's failure to recognize the courtesan proved he was lying. Another model is 'privacy protection' - while solving the case, also protecting the privacy and dignity of those involved.

🏛 The Outcome

The thief did not recognize the courtesan, yet exclaimed 'You invited me to treat an illness, and now you arrest me as a thief?' - this exposed his lie. The magistrate laughed, and the thief confessed. This case demonstrated the value of 'identity verification' in exposing lies, and also reflected the veteran clerk's compassion - he wanted to solve the case while protecting the bride's dignity.

💛 Why This Matters

The veteran clerk's wisdom and compassion are admirable - he wanted to solve the case while also protecting the bride's dignity. The thief's lie nearly succeeded, but was ultimately exposed by the veteran clerk's wisdom. The story teaches us: in solving cases, protecting the dignity of those involved is as important as pursuing the truth.

🔮 The Deeper Lesson

The underlying logic is 'knowing information does not equal knowing a person' - although the thief knew the bride's intimate information, he did not know the bride personally. Through identity verification, one can distinguish between 'knowing information' and 'knowing a person.' Extending to the modern era: witness identification, suspect recognition, and identity verification all follow the same logic - confirming identity through direct observation.
