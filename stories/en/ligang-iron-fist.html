LEVEL 07 · MONEY & WEALTH

Li Gang (李纲)

Iron-Fist Order in Wartime

Life Principle
When facing multiple crises, stabilize the interior first before confronting the exterior. Show no mercy to those who exploit chaos for personal gain — otherwise, you will lose everything.

⏳ The Setting

During the Jingkang Incident, Jin dynasty forces besieged the Song capital of Bianjing. Inside the walls, panic spread. Criminals took advantage of the chaos to rob palace attendants of their gold and silk, then turned in the stolen weapons to the authorities, posing as patriotic defenders of the city. Law and order were collapsing.

⚖ The Dilemma

While the enemy besieged the city from outside, criminals were plundering from within. Worse, they were disguising their crimes as heroism — handing over stolen military equipment and claiming rewards. If this wasn't stopped immediately, the city would descend into anarchy, and the defense would collapse from the inside before the enemy ever breached the walls.

💡 The Strategy

Li Gang saw through the deception instantly. He ordered all those who had 'contributed captured weapons' to report to the Military Defense Office, where each of the twenty-plus individuals registered their names. Once they had all identified themselves — thinking they were about to be rewarded — Li Gang had every single one of them executed on the spot. The message was unmistakable: looting during a siege is not patriotism — it is treason.

🏛 The Outcome

Despite a prolonged siege lasting over a month, there was not a single case of theft or disorder within the city. Internal stability allowed the defenders to focus entirely on the external threat, and the city's defenses held.

💛 Why This Matters

This is not a comfortable story. Twenty-plus lives ended in a single stroke of judgment. But it forces us to ask hard questions about leadership in crisis: When the survival of thousands depends on order, what price is acceptable to maintain it? Li Gang's decision was brutal — but it prevented a far greater catastrophe. The criminals had calculated that chaos was their cover. Li Gang showed them that in a true crisis, there is no cover.

🔮 The Deeper Lesson

The underlying principle is what modern theorists call the 'broken window effect' — if minor crimes go unpunished, they signal that disorder is acceptable, which triggers a cascade of increasingly serious crimes. In a siege environment, this cascade could be fatal. Li Gang understood that internal order is the foundation of external defense. This lesson extends to any leadership situation: when facing multiple crises simultaneously, stabilize the foundation first. You cannot fight an external enemy while your own house is burning. And sometimes, the most compassionate thing a leader can do for the many is to be merciless toward the few who would destroy the whole.

Reflection

1 Li Gang executed over twenty people during a siege to maintain order. When does the need for order justify extreme measures? Where is the ethical boundary?
2 The criminals tried to disguise robbery as patriotic action by handing over stolen weapons to the authorities. Can you think of modern examples where people use noble-sounding justifications to cover selfish behavior?
3 In a crisis, Li Gang prioritized internal stability before fighting the external enemy. How does this principle apply to managing a team, a company, or even your own life during turbulent times?
