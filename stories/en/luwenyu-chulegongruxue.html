LEVEL 02 · LEARNING & EDUCATION

Lu Wenyu (陆文裕)

Expelling the Musician's Son to Protect the Academy

Life Principle
The dignity of education outweighs the pride of the powerful. Stand by principle regardless of who opposes you. Better an academy with one fewer student than one whose integrity has been compromised.

⏳ The Setting

During the Ming Dynasty, Lu Wenyu (陆文裕) served as the Provincial Education Commissioner (提学) of Shanxi, responsible for overseeing local schools and academies. The Prince of Jin (晋王) had a favored musician whose son had been enrolled in the academy by Lu Wenyu's predecessor — a deputy who had bent the rules to curry favor with the prince. When Lu Wenyu took office, he immediately issued an order to expel the boy from the academy.

⚖ The Dilemma

Lu Wenyu faced a classic conflict between institutional integrity and political power. The musician was the prince's favorite — expelling his son meant directly defying a powerful royal patron. The precedent had already been set: a previous official had admitted the boy, and reversing that decision would embarrass both the prince and the predecessor. Yet if Lu Wenyu let the enrollment stand, the academy's credibility would be undermined. A school that admits students based on political connections rather than merit ceases to be a school at all.

💡 The Strategy

Lu Wenyu's approach was swift and uncompromising. First, upon taking office, he immediately issued a formal order to expel the musician's son — acting before anyone could lobby against it. Second, when the Prince of Jin personally appealed to him multiple times ("再四与言"), Lu Wenyu refused each time with unwavering resolve ("坚意不从"). Third, when pressed for his reasoning, he articulated a principle that became legendary: "Rather that the academy have one fewer student than allow one person to corrupt it" (宁可学宫少一人，不可以一人污学宫). He framed the issue not as a personal conflict with the prince, but as a matter of the academy's sacred purpose.

🏛 The Outcome

Lu Wenyu prevailed. The musician's son was expelled, and the academy's integrity was preserved. The incident became a celebrated example of an educational official using reason to resist political interference ("以理拒权"). Lu Wenyu's principled stand set a lasting precedent for later educators, demonstrating that the dignity of learning could withstand even the pressure of royalty.

💛 Why This Matters

What moves us about this story is the quiet power of a single person standing against an entire power structure. Lu Wenyu did not have an army, a faction, or political allies. He had only his conviction that education must remain above the reach of privilege. His famous words — "better one fewer student than one stain on the academy" — cut through all political calculation with the clarity of a blade. In a world where institutions routinely sacrifice their principles to accommodate the powerful, Lu Wenyu's refusal reminds us that some things are worth defending, even at personal cost.

🔮 The Deeper Lesson

The underlying logic is that institutional integrity is the foundation of institutional authority. A school's reputation rests on the belief that it operates by merit. Once that belief is broken — once people know that admission can be bought through political connections — the institution loses its reason for existing. Lu Wenyu understood that allowing one politically motivated enrollment would set a precedent that could eventually hollow out the entire system. In modern terms, this is the "slippery slope" of institutional corruption: each small concession to power makes the next one easier, until the institution is unrecognizable.

Reflection

1 In your own experience, have you seen an institution compromise its standards to accommodate a powerful individual? What were the long-term consequences?
2 Lu Wenyu's predecessor gave in to the prince's pressure. What makes it easier to say "yes" to power today, and what would it take to say "no"?
3 Is there a principle in your professional life that you would defend even under intense pressure from someone with authority over you? What gives you the courage — or holds you back?
