LEVEL 11 · HEALTH & AGING

延安老军校

—危言镇心安军

Life Principle
In times of crisis, confidence matters more than strategy. Sometimes, a declaration backed by one's own credibility is more effective than a hundred clever schemes. But the prerequisite is: your judgment must be based on real experience and ability; otherwise, it is merely reckless overconfidence.

⏳ The Setting

During the Northern Song Dynasty, the northwestern frontier was frequently harassed by the Dangxiang (Western Xia). As a key border defense stronghold, Yan'an repeatedly faced the danger of siege. When the court official Fan Yong (范雍) was stationed in Yan'an, the Dangxiang army besieged the city for seven days, with the situation reaching a critical point of near-collapse. The border garrison's morale was desperately low, and someone was urgently needed to steady the troops' hearts.

⚖ The Dilemma

The Dangxiang besieged the city for seven days. The defenses repeatedly teetered on the brink of collapse, the commanding officer Fan Yong wore his anxiety on his face, and the entire army's morale had plummeted. Under such extreme pressure, if no one stepped forward to stabilize morale, the city might fall from internal panic. The dilemma was: how to keep the entire army confident and holding on when the situation was genuinely dire?

🎯 Motives

The veteran sergeant's goal was clear - stabilize troop morale and have everyone continue defending the city. His motivation was not seeking merit or reward, but based on his practical experience of having endured multiple sieges: the enemy's siege capabilities were limited, and the city would not fall. He chose the most direct method - staking his own life - to convey confidence.

🧩 The Framework

The veteran sergeant's cognitive framework was 'experience-based judgment plus life-staking declaration.' He judged from his experience of multiple sieges that the situation was manageable, but experience alone would not convince others, so he added the pledge 'if the city falls, I willingly accept execution.' This 'staking one's life as guarantee' approach bound personal credibility to the judgment, enormously enhancing persuasiveness.

🧠 The Mindset

The veteran sergeant appeared brash but was in fact calm and rational. He precisely assessed two possibilities: if the city held, he would receive great rewards and promotion; if it fell, he would die anyway, with or without the pledge. This 'either way I am dead' logic gave him the courage to make what seemed like a mad promise. Fan Yong was caught between anxiety and helplessness, desperately needing just such a 'reassurance pill.'

💡 The Strategy

The veteran sergeant introduced himself as a frontier native who had endured many sieges, and directly staked his life guaranteeing the city would not fall. The brilliance of this strategy lay in this: he was not comforting everyone with 'it will be fine,' but rather saying 'if anything goes wrong, I die first.' Placing himself in the most dangerous position was more persuasive than any grand rhetoric. Fan Yong accepted his declaration, and troop morale stabilized.

🔬 The Mental Model

'Staking one's life as a credit endorsement' - when verbal judgment cannot convince others, using the most extreme method (staking one's life) to prove one's confidence. The core of this model is: people believe someone who dares to risk their life is more credible than someone who merely talks. The veteran sergeant was not gambling with his life; he was gambling with his credibility.

🏛 The Outcome

The city was ultimately preserved, and the veteran sergeant received great rewards and promotion. This story became a classic case of 'using bold words to stabilize hearts' - in extreme crisis, sometimes the most unreasonable declaration is the most effective. The veteran sergeant's strategy was not about solving a military problem, but about solving a psychological one.

💛 Why This Matters

An ordinary veteran sergeant stepped forward when the entire army was in panic, staking his life to stabilize morale. This spirit of 'though humble in station, never forgetting concern for the nation,' along with his calm post-crisis explanation of 'just wanting to steady people's hearts,' is both admirable and touching.

🔮 The Deeper Lesson

The core is 'signaling theory' - in situations of information asymmetry, how do you make others believe your judgment? The answer is 'sending a costly signal.' The veteran sergeant's 'staking his life' was a high-cost signal; precisely because the cost was enormous (in theory), its credibility was extremely high. 'Unconditional refund guarantees' in modern commerce and 'diagnostic warranties' in medicine are extensions of this same principle.
