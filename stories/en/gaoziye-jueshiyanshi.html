LEVEL 01 · FAMILY OF ORIGIN

Gao Ziye (高子业): Digging Up the Room to Expose Murder

— Common Sense Against a Perfect Crime

Life Principle
The most elaborate lie cannot survive a single question that begins with "Does that make sense?" Don't be overwhelmed by complex narratives — return to the simplest common sense. And remember: reasoning is the starting point, but action is what brings the conclusion to life.

⏳ The Setting

During the Ming Dynasty, a man named Jiang Gao (江槔) secretly murdered two clan members — Jiang Zi (江孜) and others — and concealed their bodies. Years later, he orchestrated a lawsuit through his wife's family, accusing another clansman, Jiang Ji (江楫), of the murders, while simultaneously tying the case to a property dispute. In a separate case, Wu Shijie (吴世杰) falsely accused a clansman of robbery, leading to the torture and death of over twenty innocent people. Gao Ziye (高子业) was assigned to review both cases.

⚖ The Dilemma

Both cases were designed to look airtight. Jiang Gao had buried the bodies and created a plausible narrative pointing to someone else. Wu Shijie had weaponized the legal system itself, using the gravity of robbery charges to destroy his enemies through forced confessions. Previous officials had accepted these surface stories without question. Gao Ziye needed to see through crimes that had been engineered to be invisible.

💡 The Strategy

In the Jiang Gao case, Gao Ziye asked one devastating question: "He killed someone and buried the bodies in his own room — does that make any sense?" (为尔争宅址杀人埋尸己室，有斯理乎？) A real murderer would hide the evidence far from his own home, not under his own floor. This single common-sense objection shattered the entire frame-up. Gao Ziye ordered a search of Jiang Gao's house, and the two bodies were unearthed. In the Wu Shijie case, Gao Ziye noticed a different kind of contradiction: Wu Shijiang, the accused, was a man of property — "he had fields and a house." Why would a wealthy man rob someone for "a cloth skirt and a few measures of grain"? The mismatch between the suspect's means and the petty value of the alleged loot exposed the accusation as a fabrication.

🏛 The Outcome

Jiang Gao's room was excavated, the two bodies were found, and his conspiracy collapsed completely. Wu Shijiang's case was reopened for review. Both cases demonstrated the power of simple logical interrogation against elaborate deception. Over twenty lives that had been lost to false accusations might have been saved if someone had asked these questions sooner.

💛 Why This Matters

The moment Jiang Gao's house was dug up and two bodies emerged from beneath the floor is cinematic in its horror and justice. A man who killed his own relatives, buried them in his own home, and then blamed someone else — the sheer audacity of it is breathtaking. Gao Ziye's "Does that make sense?" is the kind of question that anyone could have asked, but no one did. Sometimes the most powerful tool in the world is the courage to state the obvious.

🔮 The Deeper Lesson

The underlying principle is that every narrative must be internally consistent. Lies, no matter how carefully constructed, contain logical contradictions — because the liar is building from a conclusion backward, not from evidence forward. Gao Ziye's method is what modern analysts call "narrative auditing": testing a story against basic common sense. Fact-checkers, forensic accountants, and anti-fraud investigators all use the same approach — look for the place where the story doesn't make sense. That crack is where the truth hides.

Reflection

1 When you hear a complex story, do you instinctively test it for internal consistency? What questions do you ask?
2 Have you ever been in a situation where the "official story" didn't add up, but you hesitated to question it? What held you back?
3 Gao Ziye combined logical reasoning with immediate action — he didn't just argue, he ordered a search. How do you balance analysis with decisive action in your own life?
