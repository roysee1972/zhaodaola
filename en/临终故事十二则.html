LEVEL 12 · FINAL LEGACY

Twelve Deathbed Stories

Twelve famous figures, twelve ways of saying goodbye.
Their last seconds were more honest than a lifetime.
Most of what we say in life is meant for others to hear.
Only the last words are usually spoken to ourselves.
These twelve stories are not meant to move you — they're meant to mirror you.
After reading them, you might wonder: if this were the last page, what would I write?
Nan Huaijin 2012
01 / 12
Understand
On September 29, 2012, Master Nan Huaijin passed away at the Taihu Study Hall. In his final moments, he asked his disciples for paper and brush, and with trembling hands wrote two characters — "Mingbai" (Understand). He then wrote "Pingfan" (Ordinary). After finishing, he sat cross-legged, just as he did during meditation, and passed away peacefully at the age of ninety-five. These two words were the summary of his lifetime of practice, and his final message to posterity: live with understanding, and be content with ordinariness. Understand what? That life and death are impermanent, that everything returns to dust. Be ordinary where? In the daily acts of eating and dressing, in the routines of everyday life. Master Nan spent his entire life teaching countless principles, yet left behind only four characters. Those who truly understand them probably don't need them at all.

Wang Yangming 1529
02 / 12
This heart is bright — what more is there to say?
On January 9, 1529, Wang Yangming died of illness on a small boat in Nan'an, Jiangxi. When his disciple Zhou Ji asked if he had any final words, Wang Yangming spoke only eight characters: "This heart is bright; what more is there to say?" Then he closed his eyes and passed away at the age of fifty-seven. The meaning: my heart is pure and clear — what else needs to be said? Throughout his life, he endured court floggings, exile to Longchang, brushes with death, and the suppression of rebellions, yet never abandoned his pursuit of "unity of knowledge and action." In the end, he had nothing left to prove to anyone. If a person lives with enough integrity, death truly requires no elaborate words. The problem is that most of us carry too many unresolved matters and unhealed wounds. Wang Yangming's final words were not a heroic declaration — they were an audit report on his own life.
Source: Wikipedia · Wang Shouren
Master Hongyi 1942
03 / 12
Grief and joy intertwined
On October 13, 1942, Master Hongyi (Li Shutong) attained parinirvana at the Wenling Elderly Home in Quanzhou. Four days before his death, he wrote four characters: "Bei xin jiao ji" (Grief and joy intertwined). The grief was for all sentient beings still struggling in the sea of suffering; the joy was that he was finally returning to where he truly belonged. He asked his disciples to place four bowls of water at the base of his coffin to prevent the cremation fire from harming any ants. A dying man, thinking not of himself, but of the insects beneath his feet. In the first half of his life, Li Shutong was a dashing polymath — poet, painter, actor, musician, a student in Japan, he did it all. In the second half, he became a revered Vinaya monk — never eating after noon, wearing the same patched robe for decades. He proved one thing with his entire life: a person can live through the heights of extravagance and the depths of austerity alike. He embraced both willingly.
Source: Wikipedia · Li Shutong
Wang Fengyi 1937
04 / 12
I will die on such-and-such a day, at such-and-such an hour
Wang Fengyi, known as "Wang the Good Man," was a grassroots educator of the late Qing and early Republican era. He was illiterate, yet could heal people through conversation and founded hundreds of free schools in his lifetime. In 1937, he informed his disciples in advance of the exact day and hour of his passing, and gave instructions for his funeral. On that day, he bathed, changed his clothes, sat upright, and died — right on schedule, without a minute's deviation. He was seventy-four. An illiterate farmer who could predict his own death and face it with composure — this itself is the best proof that knowledge is not the same as wisdom. Wang Fengyi spent his life teaching two principles: "Find the good in others" and "Own your own faults." Simple to say, saintly to do. He did it. So he departed without fear.
Source: Wikipedia · Wang Fengyi
Zeng Guofan 1872
05 / 12
None
On March 12, 1872, Zeng Guofan was taking a walk at the Governor-General's residence in Nanjing when his legs suddenly went numb. He was helped back to his study, sat upright, and died. From onset to passing, only forty-five minutes. No final words, no struggle, no lying in bed. He was sixty-one. Zeng Guofan upheld the principle of "self-discipline in solitude" his entire life, keeping a diary for over thirty years, castigating himself even for "glancing at another man's wife." A man like that died the way he lived — causing no trouble for anyone. Forty-five minutes, clean and decisive. He often said: "If not a sage, then a beast." He probably felt he was neither — just a man who had tried hard. And a man who tried hard deserves a swift ending.
Source: Wikipedia · Zeng Guofan
Lin Zexu 1850
06 / 12
Xingdou Nan! Xingdou Nan! Xingdou Nan!
On November 22, 1850, Lin Zexu died of illness en route to his new posting in Guangxi, at a temporary residence in Puning. In his final moments, he suddenly cried out three times: "Xingdou Nan!" — then breathed his last. He was sixty-six. The meaning of those three words has been debated ever since. Some say it's a Fuzhou dialect homophone for "Xin Dou Lan," referring to the foreign trading houses of Guangzhou's Thirteen Factories. Others believe he was warning the court to be vigilant against Western powers. Either way, what occupied a dying man's mind was still his country. When Lin Zexu destroyed the opium at Humen, he wrote: "Were it to benefit my country, I would lay down my life — why should I dodge fortune or misfortune?" His three final shouts echoed those very words. He spent a lifetime proving one thing: some people remain soldiers until the very end.
Source: Wikipedia · Lin Zexu
Tan Sitong 1898
07 / 12
The will to slay the traitors, but not the strength to turn back heaven. To die for a worthy cause — how exhilarating!
On September 28, 1898, Tan Sitong was executed at Caishikou in Beijing at the age of only thirty-three. After the failure of the Hundred Days' Reform, he could have fled, but chose to stay. He said: "In every country, reform has been achieved through bloodshed. I have not heard of anyone in China who bled for reform — that is why this nation does not prosper. If there must be one, let it begin with me." Before his execution, his expression never changed. He chanted aloud: "The will to slay the traitors, but not the strength to turn back heaven. To die for a worthy cause — how exhilarating!" As the executioner's blade fell, the youngest martyr of modern Chinese history collapsed in a pool of blood. His death was not the end of a tragedy — it was a signal: for some people, how they die matters more than the fact that they lived.
Source: Wikipedia · Tan Sitong
Lu Xun 1936
08 / 12
Let them resent me — I shall forgive none of them
On October 19, 1936, Lu Xun died of illness in Shanghai at the age of fifty-five. A month before his death, he wrote a short essay titled "On Death," in which he laid out a seven-item will. The final item read: "Let them resent me — I shall forgive none of them." This was the last stance of a man who had fought his entire life. No reconciliation, no apology, no playing the good guy. Lu Xun had criticized more people than anyone could count — warlords, literati, hypocrites, bystanders. He refused to admit he was wrong to the very end, because he believed he wasn't. You may disagree with his attitude, but you have to admire his honesty. Most people soften as death approaches. Lu Xun didn't. He kept his edge until the very last second. That itself is remarkable.
Source: Wikipedia · Lu Xun
Yang Jiang 2016
09 / 12
Announce the death only after cremation. No memorial hall. No ashes to keep.
On May 25, 2016, Yang Jiang passed away at Peking Union Medical College Hospital in Beijing at the age of one hundred and five. Her will was extraordinarily simple: "Announce the death only after cremation. No memorial hall. No commemorative collection. No ashes to keep." She proved one thing with her hundred-and-five years of life: a person can live with boundless richness and depart with absolute quiet. Qian Yuan passed away. Qian Zhongshu passed away. She lived on alone for eighteen more years. During those years, she translated, wrote, and organized her late husband's manuscripts, turning grief into words. Yang Jiang once wrote: "We once longed so desperately for the tides of destiny, only to discover in the end that life's most exquisite scenery is the serenity and composure within." Her final choice was the footnote to that very sentence.
Source: Wikipedia · Yang Jiang
Socrates 399 BC
10 / 12
Crito, we owe Asclepius a rooster — see that you pay it
In 399 BC, Socrates drank hemlock in Athens and met death with composure at the age of seventy. He had been sentenced to death by an Athenian court on the charge of "corrupting the youth." His friends had prepared an escape plan; he refused. A citizen, he said, cannot break the law just because he fears death. After drinking the poison, his body grew cold. His last words to his disciple Crito were: "Crito, we owe Asclepius a rooster — see that you pay it." Asclepius was the god of healing. Some say he was thanking the god of medicine for curing him of the "disease" of life — through death. A philosopher, settling his debts to the very end — a debt owed to life itself. Twenty-four hundred years later, that clarity still shakes us.
Source: Wikipedia · Socrates
Steve Jobs 2011
11 / 12
Oh wow. Oh wow. Oh wow.
On October 5, 2011, Steve Jobs died at his home in Palo Alto, California, at the age of fifty-six. His sister Mona Simpson later wrote in her eulogy that in his final moments, his gaze passed over the shoulders of his family, looked into the distance, and he said: "Oh wow. Oh wow. Oh wow." Three times. No one knows what he saw. Perhaps it was the replay of his life, perhaps the entrance to another world, perhaps simply the sky outside the window. But the man who created the iPhone and transformed how the entire world communicates left behind only three exclamations — no business strategy, no life philosophy, no instructions for posterity. Just pure wonder. Perhaps death itself is something to wonder at.
Source: The New York Times · Mona Simpson
Ernest Hemingway 1961
12 / 12
Bang
On the morning of July 2, 1961, Hemingway ended his own life with his beloved double-barreled shotgun at his home in Ketchum, Idaho. He was sixty-one. The official account called it "a cleaning accident," but everyone knew the truth. Hemingway spent his entire life writing about death — he nearly died in a plane crash while hunting in Africa, witnessed countless men fall during the Spanish Civil War, and wrote The Old Man and the Sea about an old man's struggle with the sea. His fictional heroes never surrendered, but the real Hemingway ultimately yielded to his depression and his declining body. His final shot carried no last words. The shot itself was the last word. It said: some people would rather choose their own ending than be led by fate. It was cruel. It was very Hemingway.
Source: Wikipedia · Hemingway
