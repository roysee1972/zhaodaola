LEVEL 06 · MIDLIFE

Ye Shilin

—Orphans After Disaster

Life Principle
When solving a problem, first consider whether there is an obstacle preventing people from acting on their own. Removing obstacles is often more effective than direct intervention.

⏳ The Setting

In the Northern Song dynasty, Yingchang was struck by a severe flood. After the waters receded, bodies lay everywhere and displaced victims had nowhere to turn. Large numbers of children were abandoned because their parents had died or could no longer care for them, becoming the most vulnerable group.

⚖ The Dilemma

After the disaster, abandoned children had nowhere to go and would die in large numbers without timely rescue. But adoption raised legal custody issues — potential adopters feared that biological parents might later reclaim the children, so they dared not take them in.

🎯 Motives

Ye Shilin hoped to find a placement solution for the large number of abandoned children in the shortest time possible, saving as many lives as he could.

🧩 The Framework

The core issue with post-disaster orphans was not that "no one was willing to take them in," but that "no one dared to take them in" — legal uncertainty was suppressing civilian willingness to adopt. Removing the legal barrier would unleash the power of private aid.

🧠 The Mindset

Calm and pragmatic, unflustered even in the face of catastrophe, skilled at finding breakthroughs in legal provisions and using institutional means to solve humanitarian crises.

💡 The Strategy

After consulting the legal codes, Ye Shilin found a provision stating that children abandoned due to disaster could not be reclaimed by their parents later. Based on this, he produced thousands of blank certificate forms citing this law in detail and distributed them to urban and rural administrative units. Any adopter holding a certificate had legal protection. Ultimately, three thousand eight hundred lives were saved.

🔬 The Mental Model

Institutional design thinking — rather than directly assigning people to care for three thousand eight hundred children, he designed a set of rules that allowed the public to spontaneously complete the rescue. He used law to eliminate transaction costs.

🏛 The Outcome

All three thousand eight hundred people survived — an extraordinary number. This case demonstrates how legal institutions can guide and protect private acts of charity.

💛 Why This Matters

The lives of three thousand eight hundred children were saved — behind the numbers lie countless family reunions and renewed hope.

🔮 The Deeper Lesson

Good institutions do not do things for people; they remove the obstacles that prevent people from doing good. The same principle applies to modern mechanisms like tax deductions and legal exemptions in charitable work.
