LEVEL 07 · MONEY & WEALTH

Cheng Hao

—Coin Casting Reveals Hidden Gold

Life Principle
When two parties tell conflicting stories, rely on objective evidence rather than subjective testimony. Contradictions in the timeline are among the most powerful proofs. Knowledge is the power to solve problems others cannot.

⏳ The Setting

A man was living in his elder brother's house when he discovered a large cache of copper coins buried beneath the floor. The brother's son (the nephew) heard about it and sued, claiming the coins had been buried by his father decades ago and should belong to him. The man who found them claimed they were his by right of discovery. With no documents or witnesses, the case seemed impossible to resolve — until Cheng Hao applied a stroke of numismatic genius.

⚖ The Dilemma

Two parties each had a plausible claim to the buried treasure. The nephew said his father had hidden the coins forty years ago. The resident said he had found them during his twenty-year tenancy. There were no records, no witnesses, and no documents — just two competing stories with no way to tell which was true.

💡 The Strategy

Cheng Hao asked two simple questions: How long ago did your father bury the coins? 'Forty years,' said the nephew. How long have you lived in the house? 'Twenty years,' said the resident. Then Cheng Hao ordered a sample of ten thousand coins to be examined. Every single one bore the mint marks of coins cast in the last five to six years — not forty years ago. The coins could not possibly have been buried by the nephew's father, because they didn't exist when his father was alive.

🏛 The Outcome

The time-stamp evidence was irrefutable. The coins were awarded to the man who discovered them. The nephew's claim collapsed under the weight of objective, verifiable fact — and a single look at the coin faces.

💛 Why This Matters

This story is a masterclass in the power of objective evidence over subjective claims. Two people stood in court, each telling a convincing story. But the coins themselves — silent, inanimate objects — told the true story. Cheng Hao didn't need to judge character or assess credibility. He just needed to read a date. In an age of 'my truth' versus 'your truth,' this ancient case reminds us that some truths are simply true — and they're often hiding in plain sight.

🔮 The Deeper Lesson

The deeper lesson is that 'objects don't lie.' When human testimony is contradictory, look for physical evidence that speaks for itself. Cheng Hao's method — comparing the claimed timeline against the objective dating of the coins — is the ancient equivalent of modern forensic analysis, carbon dating, and metadata verification. The principle extends everywhere: when someone makes a claim, ask 'What does the physical evidence say?' The dates on coins, the wear patterns on tools, the composition of materials — these objective markers are far more reliable than any human narrative. Knowledge of specialized systems (like how to read coin dates) can be the key that unlocks the truth.
