Chapter 1

Hell Is Other People

Part One: Hell

In 1944, France. Jean-Paul Sartre wrote a play called Huis Clos (No Exit).

The story is simple: three people die and are locked in a room. No instruments of torture, no jailers, no flames. The room isn't even particularly small—sofas, lamps, sculptures, everything you might need. The only punishment is that they must be with each other.

At first, the three try to maintain some semblance of decency. But soon, lies, suspicion, control, flattery, and betrayal surface one by one. They torment each other, depend on each other, define each other, destroy each other. They want to escape but can't. They want to be alone but can't. They want to stop caring about how the others see them, but it's impossible.

Finally, one character speaks the line:

"L'enfer, c'est les autres."

Hell is other people.

This sentence became one of the most famous in the history of Western thought. People quote it, discuss it, refute it, use it to explain all suffocating interpersonal relationships. It is like a knife that slices through the surface warmth of human relationships, revealing something unsettling beneath—in the presence of others, you are never free.

What was Sartre actually saying?

Many people interpret this sentence as "other people are all terrible" or "interpersonal relationships are inherently painful." This is a misreading. Sartre himself later clarified: he was not saying that relationships are necessarily hell, but rather that if your relationship with others becomes distorted, others become hell.

But this clarification was too brief to be sufficient. What Sartre truly wanted to say goes deeper than that.

What he meant was: A person cannot escape the gaze of others.

You care what others think about what you wear. You care how others interpret what you say. You care how others evaluate the choices you make. Even when you're alone, your mind simulates how others would see you.

This is what Sartre called "the gaze of others"—it is not a physical thing but an omnipresent structure. You are defined by the gaze of others. Half of your self-perception comes from how others see you.

In the room of Huis Clos, the three people torment each other not because they hate one another, but because they cannot stop caring about how the others see them. One lies to maintain a certain image in another's eyes. One flatters to gain another's approval. One attacks to appear strong in front of another.

Every action is performed to secure a position under "the gaze of others."

And the hell lies in this: you can never control the gaze of others. You think you're presenting yourself, but they are judging you. You think you're expressing goodwill, but they are interpreting your intentions. You think you're telling the truth, but they are questioning your motives.

Between you and others, there is always an impenetrable pane of glass.

Sartre's insight is genuine. Many people spend their entire lives living under the gaze of others—

Living under their parents' expectations: "You should take the civil service exam, you should get married, you should be stable."

Living under colleagues' evaluations: "Why hasn't he been promoted yet? Is he not good enough?"

Living under society's standards: "You're this old and you still don't own a home? You have this level of education and you're still doing this?"

Living under friends' comparisons: "Look at so-and-so, they've already achieved this and that."

These gazes are like a net, wrapping you inside. You want to break free, but the net is made of relationships—it's impossible to live without forming any relationships. Where there are relationships, there are gazes. Where there are gazes, there are judgments. Where there are judgments, there is hell.

In this sense, Sartre was right. Some relationships are indeed like hell.

But where was Sartre wrong?

He was wrong in one crucial place: he took the worst state of relationships to be the essence of relationships.

Consider an analogy. Someone says "water is dangerous." Is there truth in that? Yes. Many people drown every year. But if you conclude from this that "the essence of water is danger," you'd be wrong. The essence of water is not danger; water can become dangerous under certain conditions—such as when you can't swim, when the current is too strong, or when you enter the water where you shouldn't.

Sartre did something similar. He saw that relationships can become hell when distorted, and then generalized this worst state into the essence of relationships.

In the room of Huis Clos, the three people's relationships truly are hell. But that room is an extreme setup—they cannot leave, cannot choose, cannot change the conditions of their relationships. In the real world, you have the right to choose. You can choose whom to associate with, what role to play in a relationship, and which gazes to turn a blind eye to.

More importantly, Sartre overlooked a possibility in relationships—relationships that require no defense.

What does "no defense required" mean?

It doesn't mean there's no conflict. Friends argue, spouses disagree, parents and children have friction. Conflict is part of relationships and unavoidable.

No defense required means: in that relationship, you don't need to pretend to be something you're not.

You can speak the truth without fear of being exploited. You can expose your vulnerabilities without fear of being attacked. You can admit mistakes without fear of being suppressed. You can be your authentic self without fear of being judged.

Do such relationships exist?

Yes. But they are rare.

The relationship between you and a tree is such a relationship. A tree won't judge you, exploit you, or pretend. You don't need to defend yourself before a tree.

Occasionally, you can also achieve this kind of relationship with certain people. Not because you've met a "good person," but because you've built a particular structure between you—both sides ground themselves in authenticity, both sides allow the other to be imperfect, and neither side cedes the authority to confirm their worth to the other.

This kind of relationship doesn't exist naturally. It requires conditions. It requires both parties to possess a certain inner capacity—the ability not to be defined by the gaze of others.

Most people lack this capacity. So most people live in the kind of relationship Sartre described—hell is other people.

But "most people live in hell" does not mean "the essence of relationships is hell."

This is Sartre's blind spot, and it is the view this book aims to challenge.

In today's society, there is a dominant model of personal growth: first improve yourself, then improve your relationships. Read psychology books, learn emotional management, practice meditation, cultivate self-awareness—and once you're "strong enough," your relationships will naturally improve.

This model has a fundamental error: it treats relationships as the result of growth, rather than the means of growth.

As if you must first become a better person before you deserve good relationships. As if relationships are an exam—you must first pass through your own cultivation before you can get a passing grade.

But the truth is the opposite. Growth does not happen in solitude; growth happens in relationships. You don't first become strong and then enter relationships; you become strong within relationships. You don't first understand yourself and then understand others; you come to understand yourself through the process of understanding others.

This book seeks to argue for a proposition:

There is a better model of human growth—not through self-improvement, but through dedicating yourself to improving relationships. Relationships are not the destination of your growth; relationships are the means of your growth.

This isn't motivational fluff. This isn't "everything will be fine if you just have the right attitude." This is a structural epistemological claim—built upon a framework about how the world is constituted and how humans experience it.

This framework rests on two pillars:

The first pillar: The world is constituted by four basic existents—matter, energy, information, and relationships. Relationships are not appendages between two entities; relationships themselves are a basic existent, as real as matter.

The second pillar: The world humans face is not one but four—the objective world, the mental world, the dream world, and the transcendent world. You experience different relationships in different worlds, and the transcendent world means: you can stand at a higher vantage point to look at relationships again, and what you see will be different.

With these two pillars, we can answer the questions Sartre could not:

Why are some relationships hell?—Because in those relationships, "truth" has been destroyed and defenses have been activated.

Why do some people not need to defend themselves?—Because they have the capacity to not be defined by the gaze of others.

Why can some relationships transcend hell?—Because both parties have re-examined the nature of the relationship from within the transcendent world.

How do you walk out of hell?—There are four paths. Each points in the same direction: reclaiming the authority to confirm your worth from the external world back into your own hands.

This book begins with Sartre's sentence. But it will not stop there.

We will build a map to help you understand the full structure of relationships. We will go deep into the interior of the "I" to see what drives you. We will move toward the "You" to see how relationships can become a channel for growth. We will discuss transcendence—not a solitary transcendence, but one that happens within relationships.

Ultimately, we will arrive at a simple conclusion:

Others need not be hell. Relationships can be a better model for human growth—provided you are willing to take risks within relationships, rather than hiding in the safety zone of self-improvement.

This book is that map.

(End of Chapter 1)

Chapter 2

I Can Be Kind to a Tree, Yet Loathe Certain People

Part One: Hell

Let's begin with a scene.

You walk into a forest. Sunlight filters through gaps in the leaves. Wind blows from the distance, carrying the scent of earth and grass. You take a deep breath, your shoulders relax, and the tangled thoughts in your mind quiet down for a moment.

You touch the bark of a tree—rough, but reassuring. You crouch down to look at a wildflower. It's tiny, but it blooms with great earnestness. You never thought about whether this tree is a "good person" or whether this flower is "worth associating with." You are simply there, and it is simply there. There is nothing between you that requires any defense.

Now, switch to another scene.

You walk into a dinner party. Seven or eight people sit around the table, some familiar, some strangers. The small talk begins—"What have you been busy with lately?" "You look great!" "Let's have dinner together sometime." You smile and respond, but your brain is running at full speed simultaneously: Is this person being sincere or just polite? Why did he suddenly bring up that person? Is he probing me? Did I say the right thing just now?

Your shoulders tense up. Your smile is hung on your face, not something that grew from your heart. You are defending yourself.

The dinner party ends. You come home feeling exhausted. Not physically tired, but mentally tired. You spent an entire evening's energy on discerning, judging, performing, and protecting yourself.

The difference between these two scenarios is not the difference between "introversion" and "extroversion." It is not the difference between "social anxiety" and "social ease." It is not even the difference between "good people" and "bad people."

The difference between these two scenarios is a difference in the state of the relationship.

In the forest, you don't need to defend yourself. At the dinner party, you are forced to defend yourself.

Many people have had similar experiences. Being with certain people feels easy, relaxed, requiring no overthinking. Being with others feels tense, exhausting, where every word must be carefully weighed.

But most people don't ask why. They simply divide people into "comfortable to be around" and "uncomfortable to be around," and then choose to spend more time with the former and less with the latter.

This isn't a bad strategy. But it conceals a deeper question: What is the essential difference between "comfortable" and "uncomfortable"?

Some would say: comfortable people are good people, uncomfortable people are bad people.

But this explanation is too crude. You've encountered "good people" who made you uncomfortable—they had no ill intentions, were even helping you, yet you just felt drained. You've also encountered "bad people" who made you comfortable—you didn't approve of some of their behaviors, but being around them simply didn't tire you out.

So "good people" versus "bad people" is not the key.

Some would say: comfortable people have compatible personalities with mine, uncomfortable people don't.

This explanation is better, but still insufficient. What does "compatible personalities" mean? Shared interests? Aligned values? Similar ways of speaking? These are all surface-level things. The deeper thing is—whether you need to defend yourself in that relationship.

What is defense?

Defense is not a single behavior; it encompasses at least three layers:

The first layer: Discerning. Is this person telling the truth or lying? Is their expression consistent with their inner state? Will they keep their promises? You must constantly judge, analyze, and speculate.

The second layer: Protecting. You dare not expose your weaknesses because you don't know whether the other person will exploit them. You dare not tell the truth because you're unsure how the other person will react. You must manage your image, controlling how much you reveal.

The third layer: Pretending. You have to appear happier than you actually are, more confident, more indifferent. You have to say things you don't want to say, do things you don't want to do, maintain a persona that isn't you.

These three layers of defense combined are the source of being "mentally exhausted."

It's not the dinner party itself that tires you—it's the defense that tires you. It's not that person themselves that tires you—it's being forced to defend yourself in their presence that tires you.

Now, back to the forest.

Why is defense not needed in the forest?

The answer is simple: a tree won't deceive you.

A tree is just a tree. It won't pretend it isn't a tree. Its leaves are green when they're green, yellow when they're yellow. It won't present one image in front of you while hiding another intention behind your back. It won't be nice to you today and suddenly turn hostile tomorrow. It won't say "no problem" while scheming how to exploit you.

Nature doesn't pretend. So you don't need to defend yourself before nature.

This isn't romanticism. This isn't "return to nature" motivational fluff. This is a structural fact: in an environment where discerning truth from falsehood is unnecessary, defense mechanisms are not activated.

Now let's take one step further: why is defense needed before certain people?

The standard answer is: because they might hurt you.

But this answer isn't precise enough. There are many kinds of harm between people—physical, emotional, economic, reputational. All of these require defense. But there is one kind of harm that goes deeper than all of them and is also more concealed:

They make it impossible for you to judge the truth.

You don't know which of their words are true and which are false. You don't know whether the goodwill they display is genuine or strategic. You don't know whether their attitude today will be the same as tomorrow. You don't know what position you actually hold in their heart.

This uncertainty is the fundamental reason defense is activated.

Not because they are "bad," but because they are "unpredictable." Unpredictability means insecurity. Insecurity demands defense.

There is a crucial distinction here.

Some people are "bad" but predictable. You know what kind of person they are, you know what they'll do, and you can choose not to deal with them. These people make you angry, but not necessarily exhausted. Because you don't need to defend yourself before them—you've already seen through them, and you either accept or walk away.

Some people are "not bad" but unpredictable. They might be helping you or might be harming you; you can't tell. Their goodwill might be genuine or might be a facade. Their promises count today but might not tomorrow. These are the people who exhaust you the most—because you can't judge, you're forced to defend yourself continuously.

What exhausts you is not "bad people," but "unpredictable people."

And what angers you is that your need for "truth" is being continuously challenged.

The need for "truth" is a foundational need. It is not a preference-level thing like "I like honest people"; it is an existential-level need.

In Chapter 1, we mentioned that the objective world is grounded in "truth." It is what it is—distortable, un-pretendable, un-glossable. The attitude toward the objective world is: truth is non-negotiable.

When a person's foundational drive is "truth," they measure everything by "truth"—including relationships.

In relationships, "truth" means: what you say is what you think, what you do is what you say, and your outward appearance is consistent with your inner reality.

This is not a high standard. It should be a baseline. But the reality is that most people do not use "truth" as their baseline. Their baseline is "effectiveness"—as long as the relationship runs smoothly, truth doesn't matter. They treat social masks as "normal interpersonal lubrication" and don't consider it to be dishonest.

For someone grounded in "truth," however, social masks are not lubrication—they are contamination.

It's not "choosing not to lie" but "being unable to lie." Pretending to like something is not a matter of social skills; it is an existential conflict—pretending means creating false information in the objective world, which directly violates the most foundational drive.

So, back to the opening question:

Why can I be kind to a tree, yet dislike certain people?

Not because trees are "good" and people are "bad." Not because I'm "introverted" or have "social anxiety." Not because I'm bad at handling interpersonal relationships.

It is because:

Trees don't pretend. No defense needed. Kindness is possible.

Certain people pretend. Truth cannot be judged. Defense is forced. This drain is厌恶. The connection is severed.

Those who cannot be severed (such as family members)—being in a state of defense for the long term—these are the most painful relationships.

From this analysis, a core diagnosis emerges:

What is needed is not "good relationships," but "relationships that require no defense."

"Good relationships" is a vague concept—what does "good" mean? Mutual help? Mutual respect? Getting along pleasantly? All correct, but none precise enough.

"Relationships that require no defense" is a structural concept—in such a relationship, you don't need to discern truth from falsehood, don't need to protect your weaknesses, don't need to pretend to be something you're not. You can be as you naturally are.

But please note: arriving at this diagnosis is itself already growth.

Not "first figure out your patterns, then change them." Figuring it out is change. When you can say "I need to defend myself before certain people but not before others," you are already more lucid than you were a minute ago. This lucidity was not born in solitude; it was forced out by relationships.

Nature satisfies this condition, so you love it.

Certain people never satisfy this condition, so you keep your distance.

A few people occasionally satisfy this condition, and with them you have connections you truly cherish.

But there is a problem here that must be faced honestly.

If "relationships that require no defense" are your ultimate need, and most people cannot satisfy this need, then what do you do? Spend your whole life only with trees? Cut off everyone who forces you to defend yourself?

This is neither realistic nor necessary.

Because "no defense required" is not a fixed state; it is an ability. An ability you can cultivate within yourself.

Some people trigger your defense because you care too much about what they think of you. If you could stop caring—not pretending not to care, but genuinely not needing their evaluation to define you—then the defense automatically disengages.

This is not saying you should tolerate everyone's behavior. Not saying you should befriend everyone. Not saying "everything is fine if your attitude is right."

This is saying: the switch for defense is not in the other person's hands—it's in yours.

The other person can pretend, can be unpredictable, can choose not to use "truth" as their baseline. But if you have the ability to not be harmed by these behaviors—not numbness, not ignoring, but seeing clearly and then choosing not to let it invade your inner world—then defense doesn't need to be activated.

This ability is what this book will discuss in detail later as "transcendence."

But before discussing transcendence, we need to first build a map—to understand the full structure of relationships. Without a map, you don't know where you are, nor where you're going.

In the next chapter, we will first take a close look at what kind of hell "relationships that require defense" actually are.

Chapter 3

What Kind of Hell Is a Relationship That Requires Defense

Part One: Hell

The previous chapter described what "relationships that require no defense" look like. This chapter turns it around and examines closely what "relationships that require defense" actually are.

This is not an abstract discussion. We will take it apart—what exactly does defense consume? Why does it make people so exhausted they want to flee?

The first form of defense: Discerning.

You are talking with someone. They say, "Leave it to me."

Your brain immediately begins working: Have they said something like this before? Did they follow through? Is their current expression consistent with their tone? Do they have any ulterior motive? Are they genuinely helping or just putting on a show?

These judgments are not something you actively choose to do. They run automatically, like a background program, consuming your cognitive resources.

When you talk to a tree, you don't need this program. A tree never promises anything, so you don't need to judge whether it will follow through. A tree has no ulterior motive, so you don't need to analyze its intentions.

But in human relationships, as long as the other person might be lying, this program must run.

What is the cost of discerning? Attention.

Your attention could otherwise be used for thinking, creating, feeling, enjoying the present moment. But when the discernment program is running, your attention is occupied—not by external things, but by the act of "judging whether the other person is trustworthy."

This is an invisible tax. Every word you speak, every word you hear, you pay this tax. At the end of the day, you haven't done anything, yet you feel exhausted. Because you've been paying this tax all day long.

The second form of defense: Protecting.

You have a vulnerability. Perhaps it's a past experience, perhaps it's a soft spot in your character, perhaps it's a secret you don't want anyone to know about.

In a relationship that requires no defense, you can expose this vulnerability. Not because you're reckless, but because you know that exposing it won't be exploited.

In a relationship that requires defense, you dare not expose it. Because you don't know how the other person will use this information. They might use it to attack you at a critical moment, might gossip about you behind your back, might use it to control you.

So you protect yourself. You watch your mouth and stop saying everything. You control your expressions and stop letting everything show on your face. You manage your image, presenting a curated version of yourself.

What is the cost of protecting? Energy.

Managing your image requires continuous energy input. You must constantly monitor yourself—should I say this? Is this expression appropriate? Does this reaction reveal too much?—every tiny decision consumes psychological energy.

Moreover, protecting has an even deeper cost: there is always something between you and the other person. What you present is not the complete you, but an edited version. What the other person sees is not the complete you, but a version you've allowed them to see.

This means that in relationships requiring defense, genuine connection is impossible. There is always a glass wall between you and the other person—you can see them, but you cannot touch them. They can see you, but what they see is not the real you.

The third form of defense: Pretending.

This is the most draining of all.

Discerning is passive—you are merely judging. Protecting is defensive—you are merely hiding. Pretending is active—you must perform.

You must appear happier than you actually are. Laugh when you don't want to laugh. Talk when you don't want to talk. Say "absolutely, absolutely" when you disagree. Say "it's fine" when you actually resent it.

For most people, pretending is a learned social skill. They have been taught since childhood: be polite, give face, say the right things at the right time. They have internalized this skill, and doing it doesn't feel exhausting.

But for someone grounded in "truth," pretending is not a skill—it is torment.

Because pretending means: you have created false information in the objective world. Your mouth says "yes" while your heart says "no." A crack opens between your mouth and your heart. This crack continuously consumes your psychological resources—your brain must run two programs simultaneously: one performing, one monitoring the performance.

This is why some people come home from social engagements completely drained—not because socializing itself is tiring, but because performing and monitoring the performance at the same time uses too much power.

These three forms of defense together constitute a particular state of relationship.

In this state, what exists between you and the other person is not connection but a game. Not understanding but guessing. Not trust but calculation.

The energy you spend on relationships is not used to feel the other person, understand the other person, build a genuine connection with them—but to protect yourself, judge the other person, and manage your own image.

The relationship becomes a form of defensive labor.

And the most absurd part is: the output of this labor is not something positive, but "not being harmed." You spend enormous energy merely to maintain a "safe" state. If this energy were directed elsewhere—toward creating, thinking, feeling—how much could be produced?

This is what "relationships that require defense" truly means as hell: it doesn't harm you in one blow, but drains you continuously. Not a single stab that makes you bleed, but a daily drip, a constant blood loss, until you are completely spent.

There is one kind of relationship more painful than all three described above: relationships that cannot be severed.

You can choose not to see friends. You can choose to change jobs to escape colleagues. But family—parents, children, siblings—cannot be severed.

What is it like to be in a relationship with a family member who requires defense?

From childhood, you knew this person was unpredictable. Maybe they were in a good mood today and very kind to you. Maybe they would be in a bad mood tomorrow and very harsh. You never knew which version of them you'd encounter. So from childhood, you learned two things: observing (judging what state they're in today) and adjusting (deciding how to behave based on their state).

Once this pattern forms, it persists into adulthood. Every interaction with this family member runs this program. Every meeting involves discerning, protecting, pretending. Every departure leaves you exhausted.

You cannot sever this relationship because they are your family. You cannot change this relationship because you cannot change them. You can only endure.

This is the deepest hell: being trapped in a relationship that requires defense but cannot be severed.

At this point, a question naturally arises: is there a way out?

You might think of several possibilities:

The first: change the other person. Make them stop pretending, stop being unpredictable, stop requiring your defense. But the reality is, you cannot change anyone—unless they want to change themselves.

The second: sever the relationship. This works for friends and colleagues, but is very difficult with family. Even if you sever physical distance, the psychological connection cannot be cut.

The third: become numb. Stop caring. Stop discerning, stop protecting, stop pretending. But this is not transcendence—it is surrender. You give up the capacity to feel, and both the good and the bad disappear.

The fourth: learn to protect yourself within relationships that require defense, without being drained. This is an ability, but it requires cultivation.

The fifth: find relationships that require no defense, restore your energy there, and then return to relationships that require defense to continue living.

Each of these five possibilities has merit, but none is complete. The true way out lies not in any of them individually, but at a more fundamental level—what you need to change is not the relationship itself, but your relationship with relationships.

To understand this sentence requires a larger map.

Up to now, our discussion has been circling around the level of "relationships." But relationships don't exist in isolation. Relationships exist within a larger structure—how humans experience the world, and what the world is made of.

Without understanding this larger structure, we can only tinker with the surface of relationships—learning social skills, emotional management, boundary-setting. All useful, but none reaching the foundation.

To reach the foundation, two frameworks are needed:

The first framework: the world humans face is not one, but four. This framework answers "how humans experience the world."

The second framework: the world is constituted by four basic existents—matter, energy, information, and relationships. This framework answers "what the world is made of."

With these two frameworks, we can truly understand: Why can relationships be hell? Why can relationships not be hell? And how do you walk out of hell?

The next chapter begins building the map.

Chapter 4

The World We Face Is Not One, But Four

Part Two: The Map

The same event looks completely different to different people.

You lose your job. You feel the sky has fallen. Your friend sees it as an opportunity. Your mother sees it as a disaster. Your child doesn't even notice.

You are all in the same objective event. But the world you each experience is entirely different.

This is not simply a matter of "different mindsets." It is a structural question: how many worlds does a person actually face?

The traditional answer is two: the objective world and the subjective world. The objective world is "the real world out there," and the subjective world is "the world inside your head." This dichotomy is simple and clear, but it's not sufficient.

Because it cannot explain several things:

First, dreams. Dreams are not purely the objective world—the people and events in dreams are not real. But dreams are not purely the subjective world either—the emotions and impact in dreams are real, sometimes more real than when you're awake. What are dreams? They have no place in the dichotomy.

Second, transcendent experiences. Some people have undergone a particular kind of experience—losing self-awareness in extreme concentration, feeling something indescribable in music, suddenly seeing something clearly in a moment of life and death. These experiences are neither events of the objective world nor ordinary subjective imagination. What are they?

Third, cognitive elevation. A person lives for thirty years, seeing the world in a certain way. Then one day, they suddenly see something different—not because the world changed, but because they changed. They elevated. After elevation, the objective world they see is different, and their mental world is different too. This kind of "elevation" has no explanation in the dichotomy.

So we need a larger framework.

Four worlds:

The first world: the objective world.

Grounded in "truth." It is what it is—distortable, un-pretendable, un-glossable. A mountain is a mountain, water is water. Physical laws do not change because of your mood. Historical facts do not get rewritten because of your stance.

There is only one attitude toward the objective world: truth is non-negotiable.

You may not like a fact, but you cannot say it isn't a fact. You may avoid a truth, but you cannot destroy it. The objective world doesn't care what you think. It simply is.

Most people have an ambiguous relationship with the objective world—they acknowledge it exists, but in daily life frequently choose to ignore it selectively. They prefer to live within the narratives they've constructed, because narratives are more comfortable than reality.

But for someone grounded in "truth," the relationship with the objective world is rigid. They cannot ignore facts, cannot accept distortions, cannot remain silent before things they know to be false. This is both the source of their strength and the source of their suffering.

The second world: the mental world.

Also called the subjective world or the inner world. Everyone's mental world is different. Your fears, your desires, your memories, your imagination, your sense of meaning—all of these reside in the mental world.

In the mental world, there is no true or false, only experience and feeling.

You are afraid of snakes; another person is not. Is the snake you fear "real"? In the objective world, that snake might be nonvenomous and docile. But in your mental world, it is terrifying. Your fear is a real experience, even if the object of your fear poses no objective threat.

The mental world is an enormous space of freedom. In the mental world, you can do anything—fly, travel through time, converse with the deceased, create worlds that don't exist. You don't need to prove anything to anyone. Your mental world belongs only to you.

But the mental world has a danger: if you cannot distinguish between the mental world and the objective world, problems arise. Taking your imagination for reality, taking your projections for the other person's true intentions, taking your narrative for the fact—this is the root of much suffering.

The third world: the dream world.

A fusion of truth and illusion. Not purely objective, not purely subjective, but an intertwining and blending of the two.

The dream world does not refer only to dreams during sleep. It encompasses all experiences that lie at the boundary between truth and illusion:

Daydreams—the images that surface in your mind when you're lost in thought, containing real shadows as well as imagined elements.

Inspiration—an idea suddenly pops into your head; you don't know where it came from, but it solves a problem you've been pondering for a long time. It is a product of your mental world, yet it has touched some objective truth.

Artistic experience—you listen to a song and are suddenly struck. That feeling is not purely objective (just a sound wave) nor purely subjective (not something you fabricated), but something that emerges at the confluence of the two.

Intuition—you "sense" that something is off about someone, though you can't articulate exactly what. This feeling may come from a vast collection of tiny signals your subconscious has gathered, but it presents itself in a non-rational way.

What is the function of the dream world? It is an exit—a passage from the objective world into the subjective world; and also an entrance—bringing something from the subjective world back to reality.

Without the dream world, the objective world and the mental world would be two isolated islands, unable to connect. With the dream world, you can travel between the two—gathering material from reality, processing it within, and bringing back something new.

The fourth world: the transcendent world.

This is the hardest one to explain.

The transcendent world is not a "place" you "go to." It is a state of cognition—when your level of spiritual understanding rises, all three preceding worlds change.

This sounds like motivational fluff. But let me make it clear.

A person lives for thirty years, always believing "success means being wealthy." This is a core belief in their mental world. Their objective world is also organized around this belief—the "opportunities" they see are all money-making opportunities, and the "values" they overlook are all non-monetary values.

Then one day, some event—perhaps a serious illness, perhaps a profound conversation, perhaps a book—shatters this belief. They suddenly realize: success is not just about money.

This "suddenly realizing" is a moment of transcendence.

After transcendence, their mental world changes—the concept of "success" is redefined. Their objective world changes too—they begin seeing things they couldn't see before (things that don't make money but have value). Their dream world changes as well—the direction of their daydreams and inspirations shifts.

Transcendence is not "a change of mindset." Transcendence is a restructuring of cognitive architecture.

After restructuring, the objective world you see is different, the mental world is different, and the dreams are different. It's not that the world changed—it's that the lens through which you see the world has been swapped.

Transcendence is an upward pursuit. But life is not a straight upward line.

Transcendence does not exclude experiences of descent. Sometimes, after a person has experienced transcendence, they fall again—perhaps from the blows of reality, perhaps from inner wavering. After falling, they may continue falling, until they shatter.

But shattering is not the end. After shattering, the path leads either to death (spiritual or physical) or to rebirth.

Human resilience is embodied in the ability to be reborn after shattering.

First break, then establish. First die, then be reborn. This is not motivational fluff; it is the real experience of many people. You must first lose your old cognitive structure before you can build a new one. You must first experience shattering before you know what wholeness means.

The four worlds answer the question of "how humans experience the world."

But what is the relationship between these four worlds?

They are not four parallel universes. They are four layers, nested within each other.

The objective world is the outermost layer—it is the foundation, and whether you acknowledge it or not, it is there.

The mental world is inside the objective world—your interpretation, feelings, and imagination of the objective world all reside in the mental world.

The dream world is between the mental world and the objective world—it is the intersection point of the two.

The transcendent world is at the top—it contains a holistic understanding of the first three worlds.

When you stand in the transcendent world and look at the first three worlds, what you see is different. Not because the first three worlds changed, but because your vantage point changed.

This is the essence of transcendence: not changing the world, but changing the height from which you see the world.

What do these four worlds have to do with relationships?

Relationships exist in all four worlds.

In the objective world, relationships are the interpersonal structures defined by society, contracts, and law—you are whose child, whose colleague, whose spouse. These are facts, independent of your feelings.

In the mental world, relationships are your inner projections onto another person—how you think they see you, what kind of person you imagine them to be, your emotions toward them. These may not be facts, but they are real experiences.

In the dream world, relationships appear in your dreams, your intuitions, your inspirations—you dream of a friend you haven't seen in years, you suddenly "sense" that someone will contact you. These exist at the boundary of truth and illusion.

In the transcendent world, relationships are re-understood—you stand at a higher vantage point and look at a relationship again, seeing things you couldn't see before.

Relationships that require defense are, in essence, relationships trapped at the level of the objective world. You see only the power structures, the game of interests, the social norms—and nothing else.

Relationships that require no defense are relationships that can be understood at the level of the transcendent world. You stand at a higher vantage point, see the essence of the relationship, and are no longer trapped by surface-level defense mechanisms.

This is why we need the framework of four worlds—without it, we can only tinker with the surface of relationships. With it, we can reach the foundation and touch real change.

Chapter 5

What the World Is Made Of

Part Two: The Map

The previous chapter discussed how humans experience the world—four worlds, four layers. But that was only from the perspective of "seeing." Now let's shift the angle: what is the world itself made of?

Physics gives us three answers: matter, energy, and information.

This book adds a fourth: relationships.

Matter.

Existence with mass. A rock, a cup of water, your body. These are matter. A foundational concept in physics, without controversy.

Matter is the easiest basic existent to understand because it can be seen, touched, and weighed. Humanity's earliest understanding of the world began with matter—what is edible, what can be used as a weapon, what can be used for shelter.

Energy.

The capacity of matter to move and transform. Heat is energy, light is energy, your physical strength is energy.

In 1905, Einstein proposed the mass-energy equivalence: E=mc². This equation proved something astonishing—matter and energy can be converted into each other. The atomic bomb is an example of converting a small amount of matter into enormous energy.

Before Einstein, people thought matter was matter and energy was energy—two different things. The mass-energy equation tells us: they are two forms of the same thing.

Information.

The organization of matter and energy.

This definition needs explanation.

The same carbon atoms, arranged differently, produce entirely different things—one arrangement yields a chunk of black coal, another yields a sparkling diamond. The difference is not in the matter (both are carbon), not in the energy (no additional energy input), but in information—the arrangement of carbon atoms.

Physicist John Wheeler proposed a bold proposition in his later years: "It from Bit." He argued that the most fundamental thing in the universe is not matter, but information. Information determines in what form matter and energy exist.

Shannon's information theory defined information mathematically. Quantum information theory confirmed the independent status of information at the microscopic level. Information is not merely "messages" or "data"—it is the organization of matter and energy, determining in what form they exist.

Matter, energy, information—these three basic existents have been established by physics. They constitute our complete description of the physical world.

But for a system about "how humans live," these three are not enough.

Because humans are not physical objects. Humans are beings-in-relationships.

Relationships.

The interactive structure between existents.

This definition is also concise and needs to be unpacked.

What exists between two people? Matter—two bodies. Energy—there may be heat exchange or force between them. Information—they talk, write letters, send messages.

But the sum of these does not equal the relationship between them.

A mother's love for her child can be described as a series of information: care, sacrifice, memory, companionship. But the sum of this information does not equal that relationship. Within the relationship, there is an irreducible remainder—that feeling of "it's not just these things," that "indescribable but clearly present" something.

What is this remainder? It is the relationship itself.

A relationship is not an appendage between two entities. A relationship is an independent existent. Just as information is not an appendage of matter and energy (information is the organization of matter and energy), relationships are not an appendage of information (relationships are the interactive structure between existents).

Listing relationships as the fourth basic existent is not an arbitrary generalization. It has deep foundations.

The philosophical level: Martin Buber.

One of the most important Jewish philosophers of the twentieth century, he wrote a slim book called I and Thou. The core claim of this book is that the fundamental reality between humans and the world is not "I" or "it," but "the relationship itself between I and Thou."

Buber distinguished two modes of relationship:

The "I-It" relationship: I treat the other as an object. I analyze it, use it, manipulate it. In this relationship, "I" is the subject and "it" is the object.

The "I-Thou" relationship: I treat the other as a complete person. I do not analyze them, use them, or manipulate them. I simply am—together with the other.

Buber believed that the "I-Thou" relationship is not the sum of two entities, "I" and "Thou," but a third thing that emerges between them. This third thing is the relationship itself.

Relationships precede entities. It is not that "I" and "Thou" first exist and then a relationship forms between them. Rather, it is within the relationship that "I" and "Thou" become "I" and "Thou."

The implications of this claim are deeper than they appear. It means: *You do not first become a complete "I" and then establish relationships. You become "I" within relationships.* Your identity, your character, your values—these are not things you figured out in solitude, but things that gradually emerged through your interactions with others.

If this is true, then the dominant model of "first improve yourself, then improve your relationships" has it backwards. It is not "first there is 'I,' then there are relationships"; it is "first there are relationships, then there is 'I.'"

The physics level: Carlo Rovelli.

An Italian theoretical physicist and the founder of Relational Quantum Mechanics.

Traditional physics holds that objects have intrinsic properties—an electron has a definite position, a definite momentum. These properties are its own, regardless of whether anyone is observing.

Rovelli disagrees with this view. He argues that the properties of an object are not "intrinsic" but "exist only relative to another object." An electron's position is not "its position" but "its position relative to another object." Without relationships, there are no properties.

This is not a philosophical conjecture but one interpretation of quantum mechanics. In quantum mechanics, a particle's state is only "definite" when it is measured—and measurement is essentially a relationship (between the particle and the measuring instrument).

Without relationships, there are no properties. This confirms the fundamental status of relationships at the level of physics.

The mathematical level: Category Theory.

Category Theory is an important branch of twentieth-century mathematics. Its revolutionary aspect lies in this: it does not study "objects" themselves, but studies the "morphisms" between objects—which can be understood as "relationships" or "mappings."

In Category Theory, you don't need to know what an object "is"; you only need to know what relationships it has with other objects. An object's identity is fully determined by its network of relationships.

This confirms at the mathematical level something fundamental: relationships are more basic than objects.

The intellectual tradition level: the Confucian concept of "ren" (仁).

The character for ren (仁) is composed of "person" (人) on the left and "two" (二) on the right. It is not talking about "one person," but about "between two people."

Confucius was not discussing individual virtue. Ren is not a quality that a person possesses in isolation, but the quality of relationships between people. "The ren person loves others"—love is not an internal state but a relational act.

In Chinese intellectual tradition, the position of relationships has always been elevated. The "Five Relationships" (ruler-subject, father-son, husband-wife, elder brother-younger brother, friend-friend) are not five types of "people" but five types of "relationships." Chinese culture has never understood people as isolated individuals, but has always understood them within networks of relationships.

Four levels—philosophy, physics, mathematics, and intellectual tradition—all point to the same conclusion:

Relationships are not appendages between two entities. Relationships themselves are a basic existent.

Matter is a fundamental constituent of the world. Energy is a fundamental constituent of the world. Information is a fundamental constituent of the world. And so are relationships.

But what is the relationship between these four basic existents?

Matter and energy can be converted into each other—Einstein confirmed this.

Information and relationships cannot be fully converted—both are too complex. You cannot "convert" a relationship into information, nor "convert" information into a relationship. But there are profound connections between them.

Relationships convey information—the relationship between you and another person transmits information through language, behavior, and expressions. At the same time, relationships also exchange matter and energy—you treat someone to a meal (matter), you give someone a hug (energy).

Information affects matter and energy—a piece of information can change the organization of matter (for example, an architectural blueprint determines how bricks are arranged). A relationship itself is also a form of information—the information "we are friends" changes how you interact with each other.

But relationships are not merely information. This point is important and bears repeating:

The amount of information contained in a relationship cannot exhaust the relationship itself.

You can use information to describe a relationship: how long you've known each other, what you've experienced together, what the other person's character traits are, what your interaction patterns are. That's a lot of information, but their sum does not equal the relationship.

Within a relationship, there is an irreducible remainder. This remainder is what makes a relationship a relationship, and not merely information.

Chapter 6

Relationship: Both a Fundamental Existence and a Fundamental Need

Part Two: The Map

The previous chapter demonstrated that relationships are one of the fundamental constituents of the world. This chapter shifts the perspective from the world to the person — what do relationships mean to human beings?

The answer is twofold: relationships are both a fundamental existence and a fundamental need.

As a fundamental existence.

Relationships, like matter, are part of what the world inherently is. They do not depend on human beings for their existence.

There is a gravitational relationship between one stone and another. This does not require human observation or human definition. Two stones had a gravitational relationship before humans appeared, and they will still have one after humans disappear.

There is an orbital relationship between a planet and a star. This relationship determines the planet's trajectory and is not subject to anyone's will.

Even at the microscopic level, particles have relationships — quantum entanglement is a relationship between particles that transcends the limitations of space.

Relationships exist objectively. They are part of the structure of the world, not something humans invented.

As a fundamental need.

This is said from the human perspective.

To be alive, a person must have relationships. This is not a choice; it is a biological fact.

If an infant fails to establish a relationship with anyone — is never touched, never gazed at, never responded to — the infant will die. Not metaphorically; actually die. Research from orphanages in the last century proved this: infants whose material needs were fully met (food, temperature, hygiene) but who lacked human interaction had an extremely high mortality rate.

The same is true for adults. Prolonged social isolation leads to serious psychological and physiological problems — depression, anxiety, cognitive decline, immune system deterioration. These are all consequences of lacking relationships.

Relationships are a necessary condition for human existence, just as humans need matter (food), energy (heat), and information (cognitive stimulation). Without relationships, a person cannot become a person.

These two designations — fundamental existence and fundamental need — are indispensable to each other.

Seeing only "fundamental existence" reduces relationships to an abstract philosophical concept, detached from daily life.

Seeing only "fundamental need" reduces relationships to a tool — "I need relationships, so I go find them" — ignoring the intrinsic value of relationships themselves.

Only by combining both can we grasp the complete status of relationships:

Relationships are a fundamental constituent of the world and a fundamental need of human beings. They are not an appendage between two entities; they are the foundation of what it means to be human.

Once you understand this, you can understand why relationships that require defense are so painful.

If relationships were merely "optional social activities," then a relationship requiring defense would at most be "an unpleasant social experience." You could choose not to participate, and the loss would be small.

But if relationships are a fundamental need — like food and water — then a relationship requiring defense is not merely "unpleasant" but "toxic food."

You must eat to survive. But the food you eat is poisonous. You cannot stop eating (you would starve), nor can you keep eating (you would be poisoned). You are trapped in a dilemma.

This is why relationships that cannot be severed (such as with family members) are the most painful — you cannot stop eating, but the food is toxic.

There is a common misunderstanding that needs to be clarified.

When some people hear "relationships are a fundamental need," they assume it means "you should get along with everyone" or "you should make as many friends as possible."

That is not what it means.

Relationships are a fundamental need, just as food is a fundamental need. But you do not need to eat all food. What you need is the right food — nutritious, clean, and suited to your body.

Similarly, what you need is not all relationships, but the right ones — relationships that require no defense, that are nourishing, that suit you.

Quality matters more than quantity. One relationship that requires no defense is worth more than a hundred that do.

At this point, a question naturally arises: what kind of relationship is "one that requires no defense"?

The preceding chapters have touched on this question from multiple angles. Now we can integrate the answer.

A relationship that requires no defense needs three conditions:

The first condition: grounded in "truth."

At least one party is driven by "truth" at the deepest level. This person will not pretend, will not lie, will not perform a false version of themselves in front of you. You do not need to distinguish whether what they say is true or false, because everything they say is true.

If both parties are grounded in truth, that is even better. But if only one party is grounded in truth and the other respects this, it can also work.

The second condition: tolerance for error.

No one is perfect. Even a person grounded in truth makes mistakes, experiences emotional fluctuations, and sometimes exercises poor judgment.

A relationship that requires no defense is not "a flawless relationship" but "a relationship that allows flaws to exist." When the other person errs, you do not therefore reject the entire relationship. When you err, the other person does not therefore reject you.

Tolerance for error is not indulgence. Tolerance for error acknowledges that people are imperfect and relationships are imperfect. Maintaining connection amid imperfection is more valuable than maintaining distance in the name of perfection.

The third condition: independence.

Neither party surrenders the authority to confirm their worth to the other.

What does this mean? It means you do not need the other person's approval to confirm your own value. You do not need the other person to say "you did well" to feel you did well. You do not need the other person to say "you have worth" to feel you have worth.

If your sense of worth depends on the other person's evaluation, you have handed your switch to them. They press it, and you light up; they release it, and you go dark. This is not a relationship — it is remote control.

A truly defense-free relationship is a connection between two independent people — not a binding between two dependent ones.

But "independence" is not "isolation." Independence means: you remain your complete self within the relationship while remaining open to the other person. You do not need the other person to "complete" you, but you are willing to be changed by the relationship.

This change is growth. In the relationship you see things you could not see before, understand things you could not understand before, and become someone you could not become before. This is not the result of self-improvement; it is a product of relationships.

These three conditions — truth, tolerance for error, independence — can indeed all be met simultaneously in a relationship, but such relationships are rare.

Most people live in relationships where none of the conditions are met — no truth, no tolerance, no independence.

Some relationships meet one condition but not the other two — for example, "truthful but intolerant" (truth is punished when spoken), or "tolerant but not truthful" (the other person does not mind your mistakes, but also does not speak truthfully to you).

A few relationships meet two conditions — these are already good relationships.

Relationships that meet all three conditions simultaneously — these are truly "defense-free relationships." They are the ideal form of relationships and the goal this book pursues.

But there is a fact that must be faced honestly: you cannot demand that others meet these three conditions. You can only meet them yourself.

What you can do is: ground yourself in truth. Learn tolerance for error. Achieve independence.

When you yourself meet these three conditions, you possess the ability to live without defense — regardless of what kind of person the other party is.

The other person may pretend, but you need not discern, because you already see clearly. The other person may not tolerate error, but you will not be harmed, because your worth does not depend on their evaluation. The other person may not be independent, but you will not be dragged down, because you are independent.

Living without defense is not a state of a relationship; it is a personal capacity.

This capacity can be cultivated. How? That is the subject of the coming chapters.

Before discussing how to cultivate it, we need to understand a deeper question: what drives you? What is your relationship with "truth" like?

Chapter 7

Truth: The Underlying Force Across Four Worlds

Part Two: The Map

There is a kind of person who takes "truth" as the very ground of their being.

They cannot tolerate hypocrisy. Not that they choose not to tolerate it — they are incapable of tolerating it. They cannot accept deception. It is not moral superiority; it is a physiological aversion. They cannot keep silent when the truth should be spoken. It is not that they are ignorant of social graces; their mouth is faster than their mind — the truth has already left their lips before regret can catch up.

These people are called "nitpicky" by those around them. Sometimes they are called "sick."

This chapter is not a defense of such people. Nor is it an attempt to persuade them to give up. Rather, it seeks to answer a question: What is the underlying logic of the pursuit of truth itself? Why does a truth-seeker repeatedly hit walls in the real world? Where are their blind spots?

Evolution does not reward the pursuit of truth.

This is the deepest reason, and the most counterintuitive.

The human brain did not evolve to seek truth. The brain evolved for survival and reproduction. These two goals often conflict.

Psychological experiments have repeatedly verified one fact: a slight overestimation of one's own abilities — a moderate degree of self-deception — is more conducive to survival than accurate self-knowledge. A person who believes they can do it dares to try more often, and more attempts yield a higher success rate. A person who evaluates their abilities with perfect accuracy is more likely to shrink back.

Optimism bias is the default setting of the human mind. Most people believe their abilities are above average, that bad things won't happen to them, and that the future will be better than the past. All of these judgments are statistically wrong, but they make people bolder in action, more resilient in the face of setbacks, and less prone to depression.

So — not pursuing truth is not a moral failing; it is a default program written by evolution. Most people do not "choose not to seek truth" but rather "have never even realized there is a difference between truth and untruth." They live inside an automatically running program whose primary goals are to survive, fit in with the group, and acquire resources — not to arrive at truth.

The truth-seeker is an exception. Their program is different from most people's — they have turned the priority of "truth" to the highest setting, even above survival and social belonging.

But being an exception does not mean being right. It only means the underlying program is different.

Society runs not on truth, but on consensus.

The objective world operates on "truth" as its foundation. But most of human society's activity does not operate in the objective world. It operates on a more hidden level — the world of consensus.

What is consensus? It is "things everyone has agreed to believe together."

Money is consensus. A banknote is inherently worthless, but everyone believes it has value, so it does.

Law is consensus. Legal statutes are not laws of nature; they are rules people have negotiated. But everyone obeys them, so they are effective.

Politeness is consensus. Words like "hello," "thank you," and "sorry" are inherently meaningless, but everyone uses them, so they carry social function.

Many moral norms are also consensus. "You must not steal" is consensus in some cultures, but in others (certain pirate cultures, for instance) it may not be.

Consensus is not "true." But consensus is "effective" — it allows millions of strangers to cooperate and enables society to function.

When a truth-seeker gets nitpicky, what they are touching is not some specific fact but consensus itself.

When they say "you clearly promised, why did you go back on your word?" they think they are defending a fact (the person did indeed promise). But what the other person feels is an attack on the consensus (the unspoken rule in our relationship is "be flexible, don't keep score").

The truth-seeker feels the other person is undermining truth. The other person feels the truth-seeker is undermining the relationship. They are communicating on different planes, so they can never reach agreement.

This is not about who is right and who is wrong. Each person's worldview has chosen a different underlying operating system.

The pursuit of truth is a capability, but not an all-powerful one.

If you treat "truth" as a universal standard — truth in the objective world, distinguishing truth from illusion in the spiritual world, truth in relationships, truth in expression — problems will arise.

Because "truth" operates differently in different domains:

In the domain of knowledge, the pursuit of truth is a core capability. Science, technology, scholarship — without the pursuit of truth, there is no progress. A scientist who does not pursue truth produces worthless research. An engineer who does not pursue truth builds bridges that collapse. The truth-seeker thrives here.

In the domain of relationships, truth is a necessary condition but not a sufficient one. Relationships also require "tolerance for error" — allowing the other person to make mistakes, to be imperfect, to sometimes not speak the truth. A person who pursues truth but has no tolerance for error will destroy every relationship. Not because the pursuit of truth is wrong, but because relationships are more complex than truth.

In the domain of business, truth is sometimes an obstacle. The essence of business is exchange, and exchange requires strategy, information asymmetry, and making the other party feel they are getting a good deal. A person who speaks the whole truth without reservation in a business setting will reveal all their cards. This is not honesty; it is the misapplication of a tool — using a surgical cleaver to chop firewood.

In the domain of existence, truth is the only way forward. Facing ultimate questions of life and death, meaning, and self-knowledge, not pursuing truth is self-deception. A person may tell lies in social situations, but cannot lie when facing their own soul. The truth-seeker is at their most clear-eyed here.

The problem is not "being too devoted to truth" but failing to distinguish when to use the tool of truth and when to use a different tool.

The underlying logic of "nitpicking is pathological."

When others say "your nitpicking is pathological," the statement has two layers.

The first layer is that they are telling the truth. In their worldview, nitpicking is indeed a "dysfunction" — it makes interpersonal relationships difficult, makes cooperation expensive, and makes social interaction awkward. From the perspective of social adaptability, some behaviors of the truth-seeker do indeed fail to conform to mainstream social norms.

The second layer is that the statement itself is a form of "not pursuing truth." They are not analyzing the truth-seeker's personality traits; they are using the label "pathological" to eliminate the discomfort that the truth-seeker's behavior causes them. They are not diagnosing; they are defending.

And the truth-seeker's reaction upon hearing this — anger, hurt — proves precisely that the other party has struck a nerve: the truth-seeker, too, worries whether something is really wrong with them.

Here is a fact that must be confronted: the truth-seeking mode does indeed exact a price from the truth-seeker in certain situations. This does not mean truth-seeking is wrong; it means truth-seeking has costs. One must choose soberly where to pay that cost.

The tension between truth and goodness.

There is a fundamental pattern that many truth-seekers fail to see:

The relationship between truth and goodness is not one of equality but of tension.

In many truth-seekers' worldviews, "truth" is nearly synonymous with "good" — what is real is good, and what is false is bad.

But in reality, truth and goodness frequently conflict:

A doctor telling a terminally ill patient "you have three months left" — this is truth, but it may not be goodness.

A friend wearing an ugly outfit asks if it looks good — telling the truth is hurtful, but saying it looks nice is a kind lie.

Telling someone "I dislike you" — this is truth, but it closes off all possibility.

Most people choose not to tell the truth in these situations, not because they are hypocritical, but because they have made a trade-off between truth and goodness that the truth-seeker is unwilling to make.

The truth-seeker's bottom line is "never tell a lie, no matter what." This is morally noble, but it has a real-world cost: the loss of the ability to maintain relationships through the buffer of goodwill.

This does not mean one should learn to lie. Rather, it means: one can choose silence. Silence is not a lie; it is a form of negative space within truth.

Pure strategy versus mixed strategy.

All of the above analysis can be distilled into one core judgment:

The truth-seeker executes a pure strategy in a world that runs on mixed strategies.

Most people adopt a mixed strategy toward "truth" — sometimes pursuing truth, sometimes being flexible, switching according to the situation. They pursue truth in the domain of knowledge, flexibility in social situations, pragmatism in business, and avoidance in matters of existence.

The truth-seeker adopts a pure strategy toward "truth" — using the same yardstick across all domains, all situations, all times.

A pure strategy is a tremendous advantage in certain domains (knowledge, existence) and a clear disadvantage in others (social interaction, business).

The problem with a pure strategy is not that the strategy itself is wrong, but that it does not distinguish between battlefields. A fine sword, used to chop vegetables, is merely clumsy.

The truth-seeker's priority hierarchy.

Drawing together all the above analysis, one thing becomes clear:

In the truth-seeker's priority hierarchy, truth comes first. But in reality, a person needs four things: truth, security, belonging, and meaning.

Most people will sacrifice truth for the other three. The truth-seeker will sacrifice the other three for truth.

This is not about who is right and who is wrong; it is about different priority hierarchies.

The truth-seeker's repeated collisions with walls are not because they are wrong, but because their priorities differ from those of most people, and they live in a world composed of most people.

Once this is seen clearly, there is no need to abandon the pursuit of truth — only to learn to choose one's battles.

In the domain of knowledge, in the domain of existence, in the domain of self-knowledge — pursue truth with full force, without compromise.

In the domain of relationships — pursue truth, but add tolerance for error. Allow others to be imperfect. Allow relationships to have shades of gray.

In the social domain — not every truth needs to be spoken. Silence is a more honest choice than lying.

In the domain of business — acknowledge your lack of skill, and then choose not to enter those battlefields that require a high degree of strategy.

Seeing the boundaries is not weakness; it is wisdom. A person who knows when to draw their sword is more dangerous — and more free — than one who draws it at every moment.

The concept of "truth" runs through all four worlds:

In the objective world, truth is fact — it cannot be distorted or counterfeited.

In the spiritual world, truth is self-knowledge — you know what you are thinking and do not deceive yourself.

In the dream world, truth is intuition — you "sense" something real, even though you cannot articulate it clearly.

In the transcendent world, truth is insight — you stand at a higher vantage point and see truths you could not see before.

The truth-seeker's strength lies in: pursuing truth in all four worlds.

The truth-seeker's blind spot lies in: failing to realize that "truth" manifests differently in different worlds, and the methods of pursuing it should differ accordingly.

Pursuing truth in the objective world relies on facts and evidence.

Pursuing truth in the spiritual world relies on self-awareness and honesty.

Pursuing truth in the dream world relies on openness and sensitivity.

Pursuing truth in the transcendent world relies on courage and letting go.

The same driving force requires different tools in different worlds. When the truth-seeker sees this clearly, they can upgrade from a "pure strategy" to a "selective pure strategy" — being pure where purity is called for, and flexible where flexibility is called for.

This is not a betrayal of the self. This is evolution.

Chapter 8

What Does the Self Attach To

Part Three: I and You

We have discussed the structure of the world (the four worlds) and the constituents of the world (the four fundamental existences), we have discussed the nature of relationships and the underlying logic of "truth." Now we turn to a deeper question:

What does this thing called "I" ultimately depend on?

First, let us describe a state of being.

No money, yet I long for it. No freedom, yet I long for it. Much of it is daydreaming. The conditions of reality do not allow it, yet I still long for it. I cannot help but keep longing. Longing is futile, yet I still long. I have longed for years without it ever coming to pass, yet I still long. I will probably long until the day I die.

This is not a problem that needs to be analyzed. It is an honest description of a state of being.

Every sentence has the same structure: "…and yet I still long."

I long to make money, but I cannot, and yet I still long. I long for freedom, but the conditions are not there, and yet I still long. I long to change my circumstances, but I lack the power, and yet I still long.

Desire is the essence of being human. Even when conditions do not permit it, desire persists. You cannot command yourself not to long, just as you cannot command yourself not to be hungry.

The word "daydreaming" usually carries a pejorative connotation — unrealistic, idle fantasy, a waste of time. But daydreaming may not be useless. It defines the direction of your inner longing. The thing you keep thinking about is your deepest need. It will not disappear because you suppress it. It will only surface in other ways — anxiety, dissatisfaction, a nameless sense of loss.

The key is not to stop "longing," but to learn how to live with these "longings" — whether to be tormented by them, or to treat them as part of your inner map.

Desire depends on the body.

All of your "longings" — longing for delicious food, longing to see beautiful scenery, longing to be with someone you love, longing to do something of value — require the body to execute.

Without the body, there is no sense of taste, and "longing for delicious food" loses its foundation. Without the body, there is no sense of sight, and "longing to see beautiful scenery" loses its vehicle. Without the body, there is no sense of touch, and "longing to be with someone you love" loses its means.

The body is the physical foundation of desire. This is an inescapable fact.

But this fact brings a deeper unease:

If everything about "I" — desires, perceptions, pleasures — is bound to the body, then "I" is merely a byproduct of a physical organism.

There is no possibility of independent existence. When the body is here, "I" is here. When the body is gone, "I" is gone.

This fills one with a deep reluctance.

Not a philosophical reluctance, but an existential one. You have lived for decades, accumulated so many feelings, so many memories, so much understanding — if all of this is merely noise from the body, then nothing has meaning.

What happens after death?

This question has no answer. Various cultures and faiths have their own depictions — heaven, hell, reincarnation, oblivion — but none can be fully verified by the experience of this life.

Yet this question is worth contemplating, not because it has an answer, but because it reveals a core anxiety:

Is "I" merely an appendage of the body? Is there some form of "I" that exists independently of the flesh?

If "I" is merely a byproduct of the body, then after death there is reset to zero. All feelings, memories, and understanding vanish along with the body. This is a possibility that is impossible to accept yet impossible to refute.

If "I" has some form of existence independent of the body, then perhaps there is some continuation after death. But in what form? In what manner of existence? We do not know.

This is not a question that can be "thought through to a clear answer." But it is the deep driving force that pushes people to contemplate relationships, to contemplate transcendence — because you do not want to be merely an appendage of the body; you want to know whether "I" can in some way transcend the limitations of the flesh.

There is one direction that may touch the edge of this question:

If "I" can transcend my own body to perceive another person, does that mean "I" is not entirely bound by the flesh?

This question leads us from "what does 'I' depend on?" to "can 'I' reach another person?"

Chapter 9

Perceiving Others: Breaking Through the Boundaries of Self

Part Three: I and You

While alive, can "I" truly reach another person?

This is not a rhetorical question. It is a real one.

Your body is a closed system. Your skin is the boundary that separates "you" from "the outside." Your sensory organs — eyes, ears, nose, tongue, skin — receive external signals, but these signals must be processed by your brain before they become "feelings."

This means: everything you feel has been filtered through your body and brain. You can never directly feel what another person feels. You can only "guess" their feelings through indirect means — observation, listening, imagination, analogy.

This is the fundamental human condition: you are trapped in your own body and can only reach others through indirect means.

If "feeling" refers to physical touch, then yes, direct contact is required — a handshake, an embrace, a touch.

If it refers to emotional or spiritual "perception" or "empathy," the situation is different.

We can indeed understand others' feelings to a certain degree.

You see a person crying, and you know they are sad. You do not need to be sad yourself to know they are sad. You "infer" their feelings by observing their expression, hearing their voice, and recalling your own experiences of crying.

This is not perfect understanding. Your inference may be wrong. The crying you see may be from joy, not sadness. Your inference depends on your own experience — if you have never experienced a certain kind of pain, it is difficult to understand someone who has.

But this imperfect understanding is still understanding. It is not zero.

The problem is this: other people's thoughts are not completely transparent.

You may be able to sense some of them, or you may be completely unable to. Other people's thoughts may be partially expressed, or partially disguised.

A friend tells you "I'm fine." Are they really fine? Maybe they truly are. Maybe they do not want you to worry. Maybe they think you would not understand. Maybe they are protecting themselves.

You cannot be one hundred percent certain. You can only make a judgment based on more information — their expression, their tone, their past behavior, what you know about them. This judgment may be right or it may be wrong.

Distinguishing truth from falsehood requires time, experience, and wisdom. But the first step is a willingness to pay attention and listen.

Most people are unwilling. They are too busy expressing themselves, too busy maintaining their own image, too busy jockeying for a favorable position in relationships. They have no energy left to truly attend to another person.

Here a crucial shift emerges:

What if, instead of focusing on your own feelings, you focused on others' feelings? Would life be different? Would your level of being be elevated?

From the perspective of many wisdom traditions, the answer is yes.

The psychological perspective: Excessive self-focus — especially on one's own deficiencies and pain — tends to form a closed loop of inner friction. Your attention circulates inward: What am I lacking? What is wrong with me? What do others think of me? What should I do? — these questions spin tighter and tighter, more and more anxiously.

Turning attention toward others, understanding their circumstances and needs, can break this loop. Not for the utilitarian reason that "helping others makes you feel good," but because the structural redirection of attention itself changes your relationship with the world.

The philosophical perspective: Martin Buber said that the "I" is not defined in isolation but emerges in the light of the "Thou." You can only become a true "I" in a genuine encounter with another person.

This does not mean you lack independent existence. Rather, it means your existence can only fully unfold in relationships. A person who is completely isolated is not a complete person.

The spiritual perspective: The Buddhist concept of "no-self" does not mean annihilating the self but shifting attention away from the self. When you cease to cling to "my" feelings, "my" interests, "my" image, you discover a broader world.

The Christian commandment to "love your neighbor as yourself" does not mean belittling yourself but expanding the self to include others. Your "I" is no longer confined to your body and your interests but encompasses the people you love.

The Confucian concept of ren (仁) is about "between people" — not an individual virtue but the quality of relationships. A person of ren is not simply a "good person"; a person of ren is "a person who maintains authenticity and goodwill within relationships."

Three traditions, all pointing in the same direction.

The difference between focusing on yourself and focusing on others can be illustrated with a geometric metaphor.

When you focus on your own feelings, you are a closed line. All energy circulates inward, spinning tighter and tighter, becoming smaller and smaller. Your world is only yourself; your problems are only your own problems; your pain is only your own pain.

When you focus on others' feelings, you become an intersection point. Energy has an outlet, has new input, has unexpected connections. You are no longer a closed system but an open node — connected to other people, connected to a larger world.

You may not be able to "enter" another person. You can never fully know what another person feels. But at the point of intersection, you can discover things you could never discover on your own.

There is a concrete verification of this.

Right now you are reading this book, contemplating these questions — about relationships, about truth, about what "I" depends on. You are trying to understand another person's (the author's) thinking. You are trying to use their framework to examine your own experience.

This itself is you using a finite body to do something that transcends the body.

You are breaching your own boundaries. Not a physical breach, but a cognitive, emotional, and existential one.

Every time you truly understand another person's situation — not a surface-level "I see," but a deep "I feel it" — in that moment your "I" becomes a little larger than it was.

Whether this "largeness" can be carried beyond death, we do not know. But while you are alive, it is real.

And this "largeness" happens precisely in relationships that require no defense — when you let down your defenses and truly feel another person, your boundaries begin to expand.

In relationships that require defense, your boundaries are constricted — you spend all your energy protecting yourself, with no surplus to feel others.

This is the deeper value of relationships that require no defense: they do not merely prevent exhaustion; they enable growth.

And this growth cannot be obtained by "improving yourself." You cannot achieve it by reading a hundred books, doing a thousand meditation sessions, or writing ten thousand journal entries. This growth happens only in relationships — in the moments when you truly face another person, truly understand another person, and are truly moved by another person.

This is the central claim of this book: relationships are not the result of growth; relationships are the means of growth.

From "what does 'I' depend on?" to "can 'I' reach another person?" — we have traveled a stretch of road.

The answer is: yes, but with limits. While alive, you can partially perceive others, but you can never fully perceive them. This is not a failure; it is the fundamental human condition.

But this "partial" perception is already precious. Every act of understanding is a moment of transcendence. Every connection is an act of expansion.

The question is: in what state do understanding and connection happen most readily?

Chapter 10

Three Kinds of Pleasure: From Instinct to Flow

Part Three: I and You

What truly satisfies me?

This question may seem unrelated to "relationships." But it is actually a key to understanding defense-free relationships.

Because when you figure out "what truly satisfies you," you will discover that the deepest satisfaction occurs in the moment you let down your defenses.

The first kind of pleasure: instinct-driven pleasure.

Childhood treats and toys. The hormonal surges of adolescence. The various sensory enjoyments of adulthood.

These pleasures are real. When you eat something delicious, the body delights. When you see a beautiful landscape, the eyes are satisfied. When you hear beautiful music, the ears take pleasure.

But one must ask: are these pleasures being enjoyed by "me," or are they driven by instinct?

When you have gone hungry for three days and finally eat a bowl of hot noodles, the sense of satisfaction is enormous. But where does that satisfaction come from? From your body — your stomach is growling, your blood sugar is dropping, your brain is sending out "need food" signals. When you eat the noodles, those signals are eliminated, and your body returns to a state of balance.

This pleasure does not belong to "me." It belongs to the body, to the program written by evolution. You are merely an executor.

There is nothing wrong with instinctive pleasure. But it has one characteristic: it does not last. Once you are full, you are no longer hungry, but tomorrow you will be hungry again. Once satisfied, you no longer crave, but soon you will crave again. It is a cycle, not a destination.

Instinctive pleasure does not belong to "me"; it belongs to the body, to the program written by evolution.

The second kind of pleasure: achievement-driven pleasure.

Studying, learning, the feeling of surpassing others. Scoring higher on an exam, performing better at work, earning more money than others.

The sense of achievement is real. When you accomplish something difficult, when you do better than others, when you receive recognition — the satisfaction is intense.

But achievement-driven pleasure has a fatal dependency: it requires the premise of "being better than others."

You are number one in your company, and you feel very satisfied. But if you discover that someone else is stronger than you, the satisfaction vanishes instantly. You earn a million, and you are very happy. But if you discover your neighbor earned two million, happiness instantly turns into anxiety.

Achievement is not independent. It depends on comparison. And comparison is a bottomless pit — there will always be someone stronger than you, always something more than what you have.

The deeper problem is: achievement does not belong to "me." It belongs to the illusion of "being better than others."

You think you are proud of yourself, but in fact you are proud of "being better than others." If you were the only person left in the world, with no one to compare against, the sense of achievement would vanish.

This does not mean achievement is unimportant. Achievement has practical value — it brings resources, opportunities, and the enhancement of capability. But the "pleasure" that achievement brings is illusory, because it depends on external conditions.

The third kind of pleasure: flow.

This term comes from the psychologist Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi. He discovered that at certain moments people enter a special state — total immersion, forgetting time, forgetting the self, focused entirely on the task at hand.

Writing, and losing track of time. Reading, and forgetting the surroundings. Listening to music, and forgetting the self. Doing something, and forgetting "what I am doing."

The characteristic of this experience is: there is no awareness of "what I am doing," only the immersion itself.

Certain music, certain works of art, make people exclaim that "these things do not come from this world but from heaven." Listening to Amazing Grace has moved people for decades. Listening to Richard Clayderman's piano pieces in youth, listening to Hello and The Story of Time — all of these produce the experience of "the melody lingering, resonating for days on end."

This experience is not instinctive pleasure — it has nothing to do with the body's needs. It is not achievement-driven pleasure — it has nothing to do with comparison. It is a third thing — pure immersion, pure feeling, pure being.

The three kinds of pleasure reveal a deeper criterion of judgment: measure the value of something by whether it belongs to "me."

Instinctive pleasure does not belong to me — it belongs to the body. So I keep my distance from it and do not let it control me.

The sense of achievement does not belong to me — it belongs to the illusion of "being better than others." So I see through its illusory nature and do not let it define me.

Where does "me" go during flow?

This is the most critical question.

In the state of flow, "me" disappears. Not death, not sleep, but a temporary stepping aside. Your self-awareness — those thoughts about "who I am," "what I am doing," "what others think of me" — grows temporarily quiet.

What takes its place is something larger. It is not "you" who is writing; it is something "writing through you." It is not "you" who is listening to music; it is the music "flowing through you."

In flow, something larger flows through you.

What is the relationship between flow and the transcendent world?

If the four-world model is correct, flow may be the intersection of the objective world and the transcendent world.

Your body is in the objective world — fingers tapping on the keyboard, eyes looking at the screen, ears hearing sounds.

Your mind is in the transcendent world — no self-awareness, no sense of time, no concept of "I."

And the dream world — that intersection of truth and illusion — connects them in the middle. The sense of "transcendence" you experience in flow is neither purely objective (not just tapping keys) nor purely subjective (not imagination), but something that emerges at the confluence of the two.

Flow is not transcendence itself. But flow is a gateway to transcendence.

It allows people to briefly experience what "transcendence" feels like — no "me," no "results," only pure immersion.

This experience is precious, because it tells you: there is a state in which "I" is not at the center, defenses are unnecessary, and the wall between you and the world temporarily vanishes.

What is the relationship between flow and relationships?

A relationship in a state of flow is a defense-free relationship.

When you are with another person, if you can reach a state similar to flow — forgetting "who I am," forgetting "how they see me," simply being together in a pure way — that is a defense-free relationship.

This state is rare. But once you have experienced it, you know what it is like.

It does not require language. Does not require promises. Does not require rules. It only requires that both people have let down their defenses, have stopped performing, and are simply "being."

This is what Buber called the "I-Thou" relationship. It is not two subjects — "I" and "you" — engaged in a strategic game, but something that emerges in the space "between I and Thou."

But here is the problem: flow is sporadic. You cannot command yourself into flow. Defense-free relationships are also sporadic. You cannot command a relationship to become defense-free.

How do you move from sporadic to sustained?

How do you move from the occasional experience of flow to sustained transcendence?

Chapter 11

Revisiting Hell Is Other People

Part Three: I and You

In Part One, we started from Sartre's famous statement. Now, armed with the framework of four worlds, four basic forms of existence, the underlying logic of "truth," flow experiences, and the ability to perceive others, we return to Sartre to reexamine his claim.

Sartre said: Hell is other people.

In the locked room of No Exit, three dead souls torment one another. There are no instruments of torture, no jailers—the only punishment is each other's presence.

Sartre's insight was this: human beings cannot escape the gaze of others. Your behavior is defined by others' evaluations. Half of your self-knowledge comes from how others see you. The mere existence of others is a threat to your freedom.

This insight is genuine. But it is incomplete.

Using our framework, we can identify three critical errors in Sartre's reasoning.

The first error: Sartre saw only relationships at the level of the objective world.

In the objective world, relationships are indeed power structures. When you look at me, I am defined by you. When I look at you, you are defined by me. This is a zero-sum game: your freedom is built upon scrutinizing me, and mine upon scrutinizing you.

At this level, "hell is other people" holds true. Because relationships in the objective world are genuinely game-theoretical—involving interests, power, and control.

But relationships do not exist only in the objective world.

In the mental world, relationships are your inner projections onto others. Half of the "other person" you see is a projection of yourself. You believe they are judging you, but perhaps they are not judging you at all—it is you judging yourself, then projecting that judgment onto them.

What Sartre called "the gaze of others" is, to a large extent, an imagined gaze. What truly traps you is rarely what others are actually thinking, but what you believe they are thinking.

This explains a common phenomenon: some people are extremely anxious in social situations, convinced that everyone is watching them. In reality, most people are too busy worrying about how others perceive them to be paying attention to you at all.

Half of "the gaze of others" is your own projection. Sartre mistook projection for fact.

The second error: Sartre ignored the existence of relationships that require no defense.

In Sartre's world, all relationships are game-theoretical. Everyone defines themselves through the gaze of others; everyone is an object to everyone else. No exceptions.

But this is wrong.

As we discussed in Chapter One, your relationship with a tree is not game-theoretical. A tree will not judge you, define you, or oppress you with its gaze. You do not need to defend yourself before a tree.

You and certain people can occasionally achieve this state as well. Not because you have met a "good person," but because you have built a particular structure between you—both parties are grounded in truth, both allow the other to be imperfect, and neither delegates the authority to confirm their worth to the other.

Sartre excluded the possibility of this kind of relationship. In his philosophy, "the other" is always a threat, always a force that defines you. He never considered that the other person can also be a mirror, showing you what you cannot see in yourself. The other person can also be an exit, leading you out of your enclosed self. The other person can also be a companion, facing this uncertain world alongside you.

"Hell is other people" compresses the full range of relational possibilities into the single worst state. This is not insight—it is prejudice.

The third error: Sartre overlooked the fact that people can change the quality of their relationships by improving themselves.

In the room of No Exit, the three characters are completely passive. They cannot leave, cannot change the conditions of the room, cannot choose not to interact with one another. They can only endure.

But real people are not like this.

People have the ability to choose—choosing whom to associate with, choosing what role to play in relationships, choosing to look away from certain gazes.

People have the ability to transcend—raising their own cognitive vantage point, standing at a higher position to reexamine relationships, seeing what was previously invisible.

People have the ability to change relational patterns—upgrading from "requiring defense" to "requiring no defense," not by changing the other person, but by changing themselves.

Sartre's three characters are trapped in a static setup. But real life is not static. You can move, you can grow, you can change.

Using our framework to reexamine Sartre's proposition, we can make a fundamental rewrite.

Sartre said: Hell is other people.

We say:

At the level of the objective world, other people can indeed become hell—when relationships are dominated by power, interests, and control.

But at the level of the mental world, half of the "other person" you see is your own projection. What traps you is not the gaze of others, but your imagination of their gaze.

At the level of the transcendent world, relationships can be re-understood. You can stand at a higher vantage point, see the essence of relationships, and no longer be trapped by surface-level games.

So the real question is not "Are other people hell?" but rather "What state is your relationship with others in?"

Relationships that require defense are hell. Relationships that require no defense are not.

And what state a relationship is in depends half on you—not on what kind of person the other is, but on whether you have the capacity not to be wounded by their behavior.

Sartre's original statement was a dead end—"Hell is other people," period, full stop. This conclusion leads to despair but offers no way out.

If we restate it using our framework, the statement can become a far more valuable proposition:

What turns other people into hell is not other people themselves, but the fact that your relationship with them is in a state of "requiring defense." This state can be changed.

Specifically, there are three levels of improvement:

Level one: Distinguish between "other people" and "relationships."

Sartre said "Hell is other people," attributing the problem to other people. But hell does not reside in other people—it resides in relationships. The same two people, with a different way of being together, can transform a relationship from hell into something else entirely. Replacing "other people" with "relationships" turns a dead conclusion into a variable that can be changed.

Level two: Distinguish between "the state of a relationship" and "the essence of a relationship."

"Requiring defense" is a state, not an essence. The essence of a relationship is the structure of interaction between beings. This structure can be defensive or it can be open. Sartre took the worst state to be the only state—like treating disease as the essence of life.

Level three: Point the way out.

Sartre's version offers no way out. Ours does:

You can curate your relationships—distancing yourself from those who force you to defend yourself, drawing closer to those before whom you need no defense.

You can change yourself—increasing your capacity to endure defense, so that even in unsafe environments you can remain authentic.

You can redirect your attention—shifting from dwelling on your own pain to understanding the other person's situation.

You can reclaim the authority to confirm your own worth—no longer relying on the gaze of others to define you.

When "the gaze of others" can no longer define you, it ceases to be hell.

The final rewrite:

Sartre said: Hell is other people.

We say: Relationships that require defense are hell, but relationships need not be this way. You have the ability to choose relationships that require no defense, and you have the ability to remain unbroken even within relationships that do require defense. What truly traps you is not the gaze of others, but the fact that you have handed over the authority to confirm your worth to that gaze.

This is not a negation of Sartre. His insight is genuine—certain relationships are indeed hell. But his conclusion is too absolute, turning a conditional truth into a universal fate.

Our version is more valuable than Sartre's because it:

Acknowledges Sartre's insight (certain relationships are indeed hell).

Identifies Sartre's blind spot (relationships are not limited to one state).

Offers a practical way out (choosing, transcending, redirecting, reclaiming the authority of confirmation).

Returns the initiative to the individual.

Now we have a complete map—four worlds, four basic forms of existence, the driving force of "truth," flow experiences, and the ability to perceive others. We have also revisited the classic proposition "Hell is other people" and elevated it using our framework.

Chapter 12

True Transcendence and Protective Retreat

Part Four: Transcendence

There is a state that looks like transcendence on the outside, but is not transcendence on the inside.

Recognizing this state is the first step toward genuine transcendence. If you mistake retreat for transcendence, you will remain stuck in place while believing you have moved forward.

What is protective retreat?

A person once pursued achievement—pursuing reading volume, pursuing goals, pursuing results. Later, they discovered that pursuing results made them anxious, made them suffer, made them lose themselves. So they abandoned the pursuit of results and retreated to a middle ground of "enjoying the process."

They told themselves: "I don't care about results anymore. I only care about the process. The act of doing this thing is meaning enough."

On the surface, this looks like wisdom. Like letting go. Like transcendence.

But one question must be asked: If you truly don't care about results, why are you still dissatisfied when the platform doesn't recommend your good article?

If you truly don't care about results, why are you still uncomfortable when you see others doing better? If you truly don't care about results, why are you still angry when your efforts go unrewarded?

The answer is: there is still a standard inside you.

Good things should be seen. Truth should be acknowledged. Effort should be rewarded. This is not "not caring"—this is "caring but pretending not to care."

This standard is consistent with the underlying need for "truth"—good things are good things, and that is an objective fact. If objective facts are not acknowledged (good articles are not recommended), then "truth" is violated.

So it is not that you don't care about results. You are angry that "results do not correspond to truth."

It is only that after this anger was repeatedly crushed by reality, you chose to retreat into the process to protect yourself.

This is protective retreat: I cannot get the results I want, so I tell myself results don't matter.

The outward appearance of protective retreat is almost identical to that of true transcendence.

Neither pursues external success.

Both emphasize the value of the process.

Both appear indifferent to external evaluation.

Both display a kind of calm and composure.

But their inner nature is completely different.

True transcendence is: I do not need results to confirm that what I am doing has value, because I already know it has value.

A truly transcendental person writes a good article, and the platform does not recommend it. Their feeling is "what a pity"—a good thing was not seen by more people. That is the world's loss, not theirs.

Protective retreat is: I cannot get the results I want, so I tell myself results don't matter.

A person in retreat writes a good article, and the platform does not recommend it. Their true feeling is anger—but they do not permit themselves to be angry, because they have already "let go." So the anger is suppressed deeper and surfaces in other ways—resentment toward the system, disappointment with the world, doubt about themselves.

The method of distinguishing them is simple: When results are not what you hoped, is your inner reaction "what a pity" or "anger"?

"What a pity" is a judgment from the objective world—good things should be seen, and it is a pity when they are not. This is an objective evaluation of facts, not involving self-worth.

"Anger" is the objective world invading the mental world—good things were not seen, and this is not only an objective fact but also a negation of your personal value. You are angry because your true worth has not been acknowledged by the external world.

If the reaction is "what a pity," you have transcended.

If the reaction is "anger," you are still in retreat—your "not caring" is false, and your sense of worth still depends on external confirmation.

The difference between retreat and transcendence can be illustrated with a spatial metaphor.

Retreat is horizontal movement—walking from the battlefield to the garden.

You left the environment that caused you pain and went to a more comfortable place. You no longer face the opponents that made you angry, no longer experience the results that disappointed you. Your life has become more peaceful.

But you have not changed. You have merely moved to a different place. If you return to your original environment, you will once again be angry, once again disappointed, once again in pain. Because your inner structure has not changed—you still need the external world to confirm your worth.

Transcendence is vertical movement—still on the battlefield, but viewing it from a mountaintop.

You have not left your original environment. You are still facing those opponents, still experiencing those results. But you stand at a higher position and see a fuller picture.

From the mountaintop, looking down at the battlefield, the things that made you angry at ground level become smaller. They have not disappeared—they simply no longer fill your entire field of vision.

You have not escaped the problem. You have changed your relationship to the problem.

Transcendence is not fleeing the objective world. Transcendence is re-examining the objective world from a higher perspective.

Do not withdraw from things—change your relationship to things.

A transcendental person does not stop doing things. They are still doing them. But their motivation has changed—not for results, not for recognition, not to prove themselves. It is because the thing itself has value, and they see that value.

A transcendental person does not stop caring about relationships. They still care. But their way of relating has changed—not for security, not for belonging, not for approval. It is because relationships themselves have value, and they have the ability to remain authentic within relationships.

But how does one achieve transcendence?

Transcendence is not a switch you flip once. It is a process—requiring practice, awareness, and sustained effort.

Moreover, transcendence is not a one-time event. You may transcend at one moment, only to fall back later. Real-world blows, emotional fluctuations, changes in circumstances—all can pull you back from a state of transcendence.

What matters is this: you know what transcendence feels like. You know you have been there before. You know how to return.

The next chapter offers four concrete paths. Each points in the same direction: reclaiming the authority to confirm your worth from the external world and bringing it back into your own hands.

(End of Chapter 12)

Chapter 13

Four Paths to Transcendence

Part Four: Transcendence

The previous chapter distinguished true transcendence from protective retreat. This chapter offers four concrete paths.

All four paths point in the same direction: rather than first improving yourself and then improving relationships, you achieve growth through the practice of relationships.

Path One: Transform "Truth" from an External Demand to an Internal Affirmation

The need for "truth" has an outward projection—

You hope good articles will be recommended by the platform—because good things should be seen, and that is "truth."

You hope truth will be acknowledged—because facts are facts, and that is "truth."

You hope people who keep their word exist—because promises should be kept, and that is "truth."

You hope effort will be rewarded—because cause and effect should correspond, and that is "truth."

These "hopes" are not wrong in themselves. But they make inner peace dependent on how the external world behaves.

If the platform recommends your work, you are at peace. If it does not, you are angry. If others keep their word, you are reassured. If they break their promises, you collapse.

Your inner state has been taken over by the external world.

True transcendence is: you already know it is good; you do not need the platform to tell you.

When you write a good article, you know it is good. When the platform does not recommend it, your feeling is "what a pity," not "anger."

"What a pity" is a judgment about the objective world—a good thing went unseen, and that is a pity.

"Anger" is the objective world invading the mental world—a good thing went unseen, and that negates my worth.

The ability to distinguish between these two is the beginning of transcendence.

Practice method: Each time you feel angry or resentful, ask yourself—"Is this a problem in the objective world, or have I allowed a problem from the objective world to become a problem in my mental world?"

A problem in the objective world: The platform really did not recommend it. That is a fact.

A problem in the mental world: I interpreted "not being recommended" as "my worth has been negated." That is my own projection.

With the four-world model, you gain the ability to make this distinction. Once you can distinguish, you can choose—whether to let this fact remain in the objective world ("what a pity") or to pull it into the mental world ("anger").

Path Two: Upgrade Relational Needs from "Requiring No Defense" to "I Can Endure Defense"

As discussed earlier, the ideal is "relationships that require no defense." There is nothing wrong with that aspiration.

But true transcendence is not about remaining authentic only in safe environments. It is about remaining authentic even in unsafe environments—without being crushed by harm.

If you can only live well in environments that "require no defense," then your freedom is constrained by your environment—good environment, good life; bad environment, forced retreat.

This is not freedom. This is another form of dependence.

Transcendence means: your authenticity does not depend on whether the environment permits your authenticity.

You are yourself in any environment. In relationships that require no defense, you are the relaxed version of yourself. In relationships that require defense, you are the tense version of yourself—but still yourself. Your authenticity has not changed because the environment has changed.

The difference is this: in relationships that require no defense, you pay no extra cost to remain authentic. In relationships that require defense, you pay a cost—but you choose to pay it, because authenticity matters more to you than comfort.

"Choosing" and "being forced" look the same on the outside, but are completely different on the inside.

A person who is forced to remain authentic in an unsafe environment is suffering—they have no choice.

A person who chooses to remain authentic in an unsafe environment is free—they have a choice, and they chose authenticity.

These two may look identical on the outside—both are "being true to themselves in a difficult environment." But one is a victim; the other is a warrior.

Path Three: Find Output Channels That Require No External Validation

If your expression depends on a certain platform—a public account, short videos, social media—then the platform's algorithm determines whether your words reach readers.

Your expression has been held hostage by a system you cannot control.

You write a good article; the algorithm does not promote it, and it sinks. You make a good video; the algorithm does not push it, and it disappears. Your quality, good or bad, is defined by the algorithm.

The solution to this path is not "find a better platform"—every platform has algorithms, and every algorithm has preferences. The solution is: express yourself on your own turf.

Your own website. Your own notes. Your own documents. No algorithm to worry about.

Write not to be seen, but to express. Being seen is a byproduct, not the goal.

The substance of this path is: severing the binding between "expression" and "being acknowledged" at the level of infrastructure.

When you no longer need to be acknowledged in order to dare to express, your expression becomes free. You write what you want to write, not what others want to read. You say what you want to say, not what the algorithm will recommend.

This freedom is itself transcendence.

Path Four: Shift Attention from "Self" to "Others"

The first three paths all deal with the relationship between "self" and the external world—how not to be harmed by the external world, how not to depend on external validation.

But there is another path that fundamentally changes the direction of attention: stop focusing on your own feelings and start focusing on the feelings of others.

Excessive self-focus—especially on one's own deficiencies and pain—easily forms a closed loop of internal friction. Your attention circulates inward: What am I lacking? Where am I deficient? How do others see me? What should I do?—these questions spiral tighter and tighter.

Turning attention toward others—understanding their situations and needs—can break this loop.

This is not "sacrificing yourself" or "pleasing others." It is a structural shift in the direction of attention.

When you focus on your own feelings, you are a closed line—all energy circulates internally, spinning tighter and tighter.

When you focus on the feelings of others, you become an intersection point—energy finds an outlet, new inputs arrive, unexpected connections form.

Many wisdom traditions point in this direction:

The Buddhist concept of "no-self" is not the annihilation of self, but the removal of attention from the self.

The Christian commandment to "love your neighbor as yourself" is not the belittling of self, but the expansion of self to include others.

The Confucian concept of ren (仁) means "between people"—not individual virtue, but the quality of relationships.

The key distinction between healthy and unhealthy: Focusing on others does not mean losing boundaries, giving without limits, or replacing your own needs with others' needs.

A healthy state is one where, while understanding others, you still know where your boundaries are. You are not "sacrificing"—you are "connecting."

The substance of this path is: severing the binding between "self" and "my pain" at the level of attention. Pain still exists, but it is no longer the sole focus of attention.

The Common Direction of the Four Paths

Four paths, one direction: achieving growth within relationships, rather than cultivating yourself in solitude.

  • Path one: Learning within relationships to distinguish "objective facts" from "one's own projections"—this is cognitive growth.
  • Path two: Practicing authenticity within unsafe relationships—this is the growth of courage.
  • Path three: Severing dependence on external validation through expression—this is the growth of independence.
  • Path four: Shifting attention from your own pain to others' circumstances—this is the growth of perspective.

None of these forms of growth can be attained in solitude. They all happen within relationships—in real interactions between you and another person.

This is the meaning of "relationships as a better mode of personal growth." You do not first become strong and then enter relationships. You become strong within relationships.

This is transcendence.

Not flying into the sky, not escaping to somewhere far away. It is standing in the same place, but at a higher vantage point.

Chapter 14

The Intersection of Four Worlds and Four Fundamental Existences

Part Four: Transcendence

So far, we have established two frameworks:

The four worlds—the objective world, the mental world, the dream world, and the transcendent world. These are the four levels through which human beings experience reality.

The four basic forms of existence—matter, energy, information, and relationship. These are the four fundamental elements from which the world is constituted.

These two frameworks can be crossed to produce a 4×4 matrix. This matrix is an analytical tool—when you encounter any question concerning human beings, you can use it to locate which dimension the problem occupies, and then decide how to address it.

The Matrix:

| | Matter | Energy | Information | Relationship |

|---|---|---|---|---|

| Objective World | Body, tools | Heat, stamina | Knowledge, data | Social structures, contracts |

| Mental World | The imagined body | Emotional energy | Meaning, narrative | Inner projections onto others |

| Dream World | The body in dreams | Emotional intensity in dreams | The symbolic language of dreams | People and relationships appearing in dreams |

| Transcendent World | New understanding of the body | New perception of energy | New awareness of information | New insight into relationships |

Let us examine each cell.

Objective World × Matter: Body, tools.

Your body is matter. The tools you use are matter. These are objective existences that do not depend on your will. Your body will age; your tools will wear out. These are facts that require no interpretation.

Objective World × Energy: Heat, stamina.

Your body needs energy to function. You need food (matter) to provide energy. You need sleep to restore energy. These are all objective facts.

Objective World × Information: Knowledge, data.

What you know, the data you command, the skills you have learned—these are information in the objective world. They exist independently of your feelings. Either you can do it or you cannot.

Objective World × Relationship: Social structures, contracts.

Your position in society—whose child you are, whose colleague you are, whose citizen you are—these are relationships in the objective world. They are defined by law, institutions, and customs, independent of your feelings.

Mental World × Matter: The imagined body.

Your perception of your own body does not perfectly correspond to your body itself. A thin person may feel fat. A healthy person may feel ill. There is a gap between your imagined body and your objective body.

Mental World × Energy: Emotional energy.

Anger is a form of energy—you can feel it surging through your body. Sadness is a form of energy—it drains your vitality. Joy is a form of energy—it gives you motivation. These energy-feelings exist in your mental world.

Mental World × Information: Meaning, narrative.

You have woven a story about your own life—"I am this kind of person; these things happened to me; this is what they mean." This story is information in the mental world. It may not be objective fact, but it is your way of making sense of yourself.

Mental World × Relationship: Inner projections onto others.

Your view of a person does not perfectly correspond to that person themselves. You project your own fears, desires, and expectations onto them, and then interact with that projection. Half of your relationship with "him" is actually your relationship with your own projection.

This is why "the gaze of others" carries such power—because half of the "gaze of others" you perceive is your own projection.

Dream World × Matter: The body in dreams.

In dreams, your body can fly, can transform, can do things impossible in waking life. The body in dreams is not the objective body, but the experience is real.

Dream World × Energy: Emotional intensity in dreams.

Some emotions in dreams are more intense than those in waking life. You dream of losing a loved one, and when you wake, your pillow is wet. That sadness is not "fake"—it is real within the dream world.

Dream World × Information: The symbolic language of dreams.

Dreams do not express themselves in logical language, but in symbolic language. You dream of a closed door, which may symbolize a domain you cannot enter. You dream of a river, which may symbolize the passage of time. These symbols are information in the dream world.

Dream World × Relationship: People and relationships appearing in dreams.

You dream of a friend you haven't seen in years. You dream of speaking with a deceased relative. You dream of a stranger, yet you feel you know them. These relationships that appear in dreams exist at the boundary between the real and the imagined—they are not objective facts, but they carry genuine emotional information.

Transcendent World × Matter: New understanding of the body.

After experiencing some form of transcendence, your understanding of the body may change. Perhaps you no longer regard the body as the entirety of "me," but rather as one part of "me"—important, but not the only thing.

Transcendent World × Energy: New perception of energy.

You may begin to perceive a kind of energy that was previously imperceptible—not heat or stamina in the physical sense, but something more subtle. Perhaps it is qi, perhaps it is "life force," perhaps it is a "sense of connection." Whatever its name, it is an experience of energy that transcends the material plane.

Transcendent World × Information: New awareness of information.

You may begin to see deep connections between pieces of information—two things you once thought unrelated suddenly connect at some level. This is not the result of logical deduction, but a higher-order cognition.

Transcendent World × Relationship: New insight into relationships.

This is the cell most relevant to the theme of this book.

Within the transcendent world, your understanding of relationships may undergo a fundamental shift. Perhaps you suddenly see through to the essence of a certain relationship—what you thought was the other person's problem, you now discover was your own projection. Perhaps you suddenly understand someone you once found incomprehensible—now you grasp why they did what they did. Perhaps you suddenly let go of a certain relationship—not because you stopped caring, but because you stand at a higher vantage point and see a larger picture.

What is this matrix useful for?

Use one: Locating a problem.

When you encounter a question about relationships, first place it in the matrix. Which world does the problem belong to? Which dimension of basic existence?

For example: You are having trouble with a colleague. Which cell does this problem occupy?

If the problem is "he stole my credit"—this is Objective World × Relationship (social structures, interest-based games).

If the problem is "I feel he is targeting me"—this is Mental World × Relationship (your projection).

If the problem is "I dreamed he was chasing me"—this is Dream World × Relationship (dream symbolism).

If the problem is "I suddenly understand why he did that"—this is Transcendent World × Relationship (new insight).

Problems in different cells require different approaches. In the objective world, action is needed (fighting for your interests). In the mental world, awareness is needed (checking whether it is your own projection). In the dream world, listening is needed (the dream may be telling you something). In the transcendent world, preservation is needed (such insight is precious).

Use two: Finding a way out.

If you are trapped in a certain cell and cannot get out, try moving to a different cell.

For example: You are locked in a game with someone in the Objective World × Relationship cell and feel exhausted. You might try entering the Mental World × Relationship cell—instead of continuing the game, step back and examine your projections about this person. You may discover that half your anger toward them comes from your own projection.

Another example: You are caught in a loop in the Mental World × Relationship cell—"What does he really think of me? Does he dislike me?" You might try entering the Transcendent World × Relationship cell—standing at a higher vantage point and re-examining the relationship. You may discover that this question is not important in the larger picture.

Use three: Expanding your vision.

The matrix has sixteen cells. Most people live in only a few of them—typically the first three cells of the objective world (body, energy, knowledge) and the relationship cell of the mental world (projection). The others are rarely touched.

Try entering those less-visited cells. Explore the symbolic language of the dream world. Explore the new insights of the transcendent world. Explore the energy-feelings within the mental world. These explorations will expand your vision, allowing you to see what was previously invisible.

The matrix is a starting point, not an endpoint. It can help you locate, navigate, and expand. But it cannot walk for you.

Walking is something you must do yourself.

In the next chapter, we return to the book's central concept and offer the final answer.

(End of Chapter 14)

Chapter 15

Relationships That Require No Defense

Part Four: Transcendence

The book has reached its final chapter.

Starting from Sartre's "Hell is other people," we built a framework of four worlds and four basic forms of existence, conducted an in-depth analysis of the driving force of "truth," flow experiences, and the ability to perceive others, distinguished between protective retreat and true transcendence, and offered four paths toward transcendence.

Now we return to the opening question: What is a relationship that requires no defense? Does such a thing exist?

What a defense-free relationship is not.

It is not "a relationship without conflict." Friends argue, spouses disagree, parents and children clash. Conflict is part of relationships and is unavoidable.

Defense-free does not mean "no conflict"—it means "conflict does not become attack." You can have disagreements without using them to hurt each other. You can argue without using arguments to control each other. The conflict is superficial; the underlying trust remains intact.

It is not "a relationship with only good people." You cannot obtain a defense-free relationship by filtering people. Because "good people" can also make you defensive—if someone is too good, you may feel inadequate and begin to put on an act. "Bad people" do not necessarily make you defensive either—if you see them clearly, you may actually no longer need to defend yourself.

It is not "a relationship with no boundaries at all." Defense-free does not mean boundary-free. You still have your own limits, your own space, your own independence. Defense-free means you do not need to pretend in a relationship; it does not mean you must expose yourself without reserve.

What a defense-free relationship is.

It is a relational state in which you do not need to discern truth from falsehood (the other person says what they mean), do not need to protect your vulnerabilities (exposing them will not be exploited), and do not need to pretend (being your authentic self is safe).

This state requires three conditions to be met simultaneously:

Grounded in "truth." At least one party is driven by "truth" as a fundamental orientation—they will not pretend, will not tell lies. The other party respects this—even if they themselves do not fully operate on the principle of truth, they will not exploit the other's honesty to harm them.

Tolerance for imperfection. Both parties allow the other to be imperfect. Mistakes are not met with rejection. Emotions are not met with attack. Vulnerabilities are not exploited. The relationship is not built on a foundation of "perfection," but on a foundation of "authenticity." Authenticity includes imperfection.

Independence. Neither party delegates the authority to confirm their worth to the other. You do not need the other person's approval to affirm your own value. You do not need them to say "you did well" in order to feel you did well. Your sense of worth is self-sufficient, not derived from the relationship.

A relationship in which all three conditions are met simultaneously is a defense-free relationship.

Does it exist?

Yes. But rarely.

Your relationship with nature is such a relationship. Nature does not pretend, does not punish imperfection (nature does not punish you for mistakes—it simply happens), does not depend on you (you do not need nature's approval).

You and certain people can occasionally reach this state as well. Not because you found the perfect partner, but because you built a particular structure between you—both parties are willing to be authentic, both allow the other to be imperfect, both maintain independence.

This kind of relationship does not arise naturally. It requires both parties to possess a certain inner capacity—the ability not to be defined by the gaze of others. If one party lacks this capacity, the relationship will degrade into one requiring defense.

What if you cannot find such a relationship?

Three possibilities.

The first: Build a defense-free relationship with nature.

Walk into the woods. Sit by the river. Watch the clouds. Listen to the wind.

None of this requires anyone else's cooperation. Nature is always there, never pretends, never requires you to defend yourself.

This is not escapism. This is restoration. After depleting your energy in defense-demanding human relationships, restore yourself in nature. Then, with renewed energy, return to human relationships and continue living.

The second: Build a defense-free relationship with yourself.

Can you tell yourself the truth? Can you acknowledge your weaknesses without attacking yourself? Can you be your authentic self without needing to prove anything to yourself?

If you can, you have built a defense-free relationship with yourself.

Most people cannot. They are harsher with themselves than with anyone else—never allowing themselves to make mistakes, to be weak, to be imperfect. They need to defend themselves even from themselves—defending against the "not-good-enough self."

Building a defense-free relationship with yourself is the foundation of all defense-free relationships. If you cannot face yourself honestly, how can you be honest before others?

The third: Build a defense-free relationship with "what you are doing."

When you enter a state of flow—writing, reading, music, creation—you and that activity are in a defense-free relationship.

That activity will not judge you. Will not exploit you. Will not make you pretend. You are simply doing, purely doing.

Flow is not transcendence itself, but flow is the gateway to transcendence. It lets you experience what defense-free feels like—and then you can carry that feeling back into your human relationships.

The final answer.

Back to Sartre. He was wrong, but he was wrong in a productive way.

The proposition "Hell is other people," with its absoluteness and its despair, forces every reader to confront the question: What are relationships really? Must they be hell?

Our answer is: Relationships need not be hell. But the way out is not "first improve yourself, then improve relationships"—the way out is "grow within relationships."

The mainstream model of personal growth works like this: read psychology books, learn emotional regulation, practice meditation, cultivate self-awareness—and once you are "strong enough," relationships will naturally improve. This model treats relationships like an exam—you must pass your own cultivation first before you can achieve a passing grade.

But that is not what Buber said. Buber said that "I" becomes "I" only in the light of "You." You do not first possess a complete "I" and then go build relationships. You become "you" within relationships.

Growth does not happen in solitude. Growth happens within relationships.

You do not first become strong and then enter relationships; you become strong within relationships. You do not first see yourself clearly and then understand others; you see yourself clearly through the process of understanding others. You do not first learn to stop defending and then build defense-free relationships; you learn to stop defending through the process of trying not to defend.

This is the core meaning of "relationships as a better mode of personal growth."

The world has four basic forms of existence; human beings have four levels of experience.

Matter is the foundation, but not everything.

Energy is the driving force, but not the purpose.

Information is the organizing principle, but not the essence.

Relationship is the structure of interaction between beings—the foundation of what makes us human.

True transcendence, in any world, means never delegating the authority to confirm your worth to the external.

In the objective world, your worth does not depend on how much matter you possess.

In the mental world, your worth does not depend on how much happiness you have experienced.

In the dream world, your worth does not depend on whether your dreams are beautiful.

In the transcendent world, your worth does not depend on whether you have already transcended.

Your authenticity, your relationships, your expression—their worth is confirmed by you yourself. Your attention can be directed toward others rather than solely toward your own pain.

This is not an abandonment of the world, but the deepest form of participation in it—because you no longer need the world to give you something before you dare to keep going, and you no longer need pain to be your sole point of focus.

Defense-free relationships are the central concept of this book.

They are not an idealized fantasy. They are an attainable state—not achieved by first perfecting yourself, but gradually reached through the practice of relationships.

They do not exist only in philosophy books. They exist between you and a tree, between you and a song, between you and a person who truly understands you, in every moment you enter a state of flow.

They do not require the other party's cooperation to be realized. They begin the moment you are willing to take a risk within a relationship—willing to speak the truth, willing to expose your vulnerabilities, willing to remain open in the face of uncertainty.

Relationships are not the endpoint of your growth. Relationships are your means of growth.

It is not the world that has changed. It is you who have changed within relationships.

Sartre said: Hell is other people.

We say: Other people need not be hell. Relationships can be a better mode of personal growth—on the condition that you are willing to take risks within relationships, rather than hiding in the safety zone of self-improvement.

This book is that map.

A map cannot walk for you. But it can tell you where you are, where you are going, and—how to get there.

The rest is a matter of practice within relationships.

Appendix B: Relationship Pattern Self-Diagnosis Questionnaire

The following 10 questions are designed to help you understand your own relational patterns. There is no scoring required, no judgment to be made. Simply look at yourself.

Question 1: When was the last time you felt "emotionally drained" in a social setting? What drained you—was it discerning the other person's true intentions? Protecting your own vulnerabilities? Or pretending about your own feelings?

Question 2: Is there someone with whom you can completely relax and need no defense whatsoever? If so, what characterizes your relationship with them? If not, who comes closest to this state?

Question 3: When someone tells you a lie, what is your first reaction? Anger ("How could you deceive me?")? Hurt ("I trusted you and you deceived me?")? Or exhaustion ("Here we go—I have to start defending again")?

Question 4: Can you manage to "pretend to like someone you don't like"? If you cannot, why not? If you can, what is the cost?

Question 5: How do you feel when you are with nature? How does that feeling differ from being with certain people?

Question 6: When results fall short—say, you did something good but received no recognition—is your inner reaction "what a pity" (objective judgment) or "anger" (subjective invasion)?

Question 7: Is there a relationship you "cannot sever"—such as with a family member—that has kept you in a prolonged state of defense? Where does most of your energy go in this relationship—into discerning, protecting, or pretending?

Question 8: When was the last time you entered a state of "flow"? What were you doing? In that state, was the sense of "I" still present?

Question 9: Have you ever experienced some form of "transcendence"—suddenly seeing through to the essence of a certain relationship, or suddenly understanding someone you previously could not comprehend? What was that experience like?

Question 10: If you could build a "defense-free relationship" with only one person, who would you choose? Why?

These 10 questions have no standard answers. Their purpose is to help you see your own relational patterns clearly.

Only by seeing clearly can you change. Only by seeing clearly do you know where to begin.
