LEVEL 07 · MONEY & WEALTH

Gao Yu (高郁)

The Lead and Iron Coin Strategy

Life Principle
Don't think about how to take money from others' pockets. Instead, create a mechanism that makes people willingly leave their money in your hands.

⏳ The Setting

After King Ma Yin of Chu gained control of Hunan, he adopted a policy of not taxing merchants to attract trade. His advisor Gao Yu then designed an ingenious currency-trade-industry linkage strategy to further strengthen the region's economy.

⚖ The Dilemma

Hunan was an inland province — without taxing merchants, government revenue was limited. Traders came and went freely, taking their profits with them. How could the treasury be filled and local industries developed without imposing any additional taxes on traveling merchants?

💡 The Strategy

Gao Yu minted coins from lead and iron — metals that had no value outside Chu's borders. When merchants wanted to leave, they couldn't take the coins with them (no one outside would accept them), so they had to spend their earnings on local goods before departing. At the same time, Gao Yu decreed that taxes could be paid in silk cloth instead of coins, which stimulated the local textile industry into a boom. The result: money that entered Chu stayed in Chu, and a new industry flourished.

🏛 The Outcome

Chu's national treasury became wealthy, and the local textile industry — powered by looms across every household — thrived enormously. Traders continued coming despite the inconvenient currency, proving that market attractiveness could overcome monetary friction.

💛 Why This Matters

There is something deeply satisfying about turning a 'flaw' into a feature. Lead and iron were considered cheap, worthless metals — yet Gao Yu transformed them into instruments of national prosperity. This is the ultimate 'turning straw into gold' story, reminding us that the most powerful strategies often look counterintuitive on the surface.

🔮 The Deeper Lesson

The underlying principle is what economists now call 'import substitution through currency policy.' By controlling the medium of exchange, Gao Yu effectively built an invisible wall around Chu's economy — not to repel trade, but to ensure that every transaction left wealth behind. Modern parallels include currency controls, capital flow restrictions, and tax incentive zones designed to keep investment local. The deeper lesson: you don't need to extract money from people's pockets — you need to design a system where they willingly leave it behind.

Reflection

1 Gao Yu turned a limitation — a currency that couldn't circulate outside the region — into an economic advantage. What limitations in your own environment could you reframe as strategic advantages?
2 The strategy of taxing in silk instead of coins stimulated an entire industry. How might modern governments use tax policy to encourage specific industries or behaviors?
3 Gao Yu created a system where traders voluntarily left their wealth in Chu. In your own work, how could you design incentives that make people want to invest in your community rather than just passing through?
