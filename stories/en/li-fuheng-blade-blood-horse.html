李复亨

— Verifying Horse Blood on a Blade and a Startled Ox

Life Principle
Do not overlook seemingly irrelevant details — they may be the key to solving the case. Scientific methods and animal instincts are both reliable tools — objective laws do not lie. In solving cases, "a killing with no profit motive must stem from hatred" is an important reasoning principle.

⏳ The Setting

During the Jin Dynasty, Li Fuheng served as a local official responsible for handling various civil and criminal cases. Social order was complex at the time, with bandits widespread, and officials needed to employ all manner of wisdom to solve cases. Li Fuheng was renowned for his keen eye for detail and his use of scientific methods, solving numerous difficult cases through ingenious approaches.

⚖ The Dilemma

Li Fuheng faced two cases: first, a government horse was stolen and killed at an inn while being escorted; second, a thief had cut off a civilian's ox's ear. In the first case, there was no direct evidence at the scene, making it difficult to identify suspects. In the second, it was known that a local resident was responsible, but it was hard to determine exactly who. Effective methods were needed to identify the true criminals.

🎯 Motives

Li Fuheng's goal was to identify the true culprits in both cases and uphold social order and the dignity of the law. In the first case, he reasoned that "a killing with no profit motive must stem from hatred" — therefore he investigated from the angle of grudges. In the second case, he used the animal's instinctive reactions to identify the criminal.

🧩 The Framework

Li Fuheng employed the cognitive framework of "causal reasoning plus scientific verification." For the horse-killing case, he reasoned that a killing with no profit motive must stem from hatred, so he investigated from the angle of grudges. For the ox-ear case, he used the method of "fire-testing for blood" — horse blood on a blade turns blue when heated, a form of scientific verification. He also used the ox's fear reaction toward the thief to identify the criminal.

🧠 The Mindset

Li Fuheng was calm and observant, able to discover crucial clues in seemingly irrelevant details. His reasoning that "a killing with no profit motive must stem from hatred" demonstrated a deep understanding of human nature. For the ox-ear case, his use of the animal's instinctive reactions showed his understanding of animal behavior.

💡 The Strategy

Li Fuheng's strategy had two parts: first, for the horse-killing case, he ordered all merchants, travelers, and fellow townspeople at the inn to surrender their blades, then used the fire-testing method to find the one with horse blood; second, for the ox-ear case, he had the ox owner lead the ox past all the local residents — when the ox reached a certain person, it startled and fled, identifying the thief. Both methods succeeded.

🔬 The Mental Model

The core model is "scientific verification plus animal instinct." For the horse-killing case, he used the scientific method of "fire-testing for blood" — horse blood on a blade turns blue when heated, providing objective physical evidence. For the ox-ear case, he used the ox's fear reaction toward the thief — animals have an instinctive fear of those who have harmed them, and this reaction cannot be faked.

🏛 The Outcome

Both cases were successfully solved: in the horse-killing case, the owner of the blade with horse blood was identified and indeed held a grudge against the horse's owner; in the ox-ear case, the ox startled and fled before the thief, who then "confessed and submitted." This case demonstrated the value of "scientific verification plus animal instinct" in solving crimes and showcased Li Fuheng's ability to employ multiple methods in his investigations.

💛 Why This Matters

Li Fuheng's wisdom is admirable — he could discover crucial clues in seemingly irrelevant details and verify them using scientific methods. The animal's instinctive reaction embodied nature's impartiality — animals have an instinctive fear of those who have harmed them, and this reaction cannot be faked. The story teaches us: in solving cases, both scientific methods and animal instincts are reliable tools.

🔮 The Deeper Lesson

The underlying logic is "objective laws are reliable bases for solving cases" — whether physical laws (fire-testing for blood) or biological laws (animal instinct), they are objective and difficult to fake. Extending to modern times: DNA testing in forensic science, fingerprint identification in criminal investigation, and instinct research in animal behavior studies all follow the same logic — using objective laws to identify the truth.
