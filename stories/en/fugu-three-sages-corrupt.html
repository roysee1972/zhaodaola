LEVEL 08 · SOCIAL MAZE

Fu Gu (傅嘏)

All Three Sages Are Men of Corrupt Character

Life Principle
Choose your friends with care. Fame and talent do not equal worthiness of trust. Character — not charisma — is the most reliable foundation for lasting relationships.

⏳ The Setting

During the Wei-Jin transition, He Yan, Deng Yang, and Xiahou Xuan were the three most celebrated scholars of the age. Known as the "Three Sages," they were the celebrities of their time — every ambitious young man wanted to befriend them. Fu Gu consistently kept his distance.

⚖ The Dilemma

When three of the most famous men in the empire actively seek your friendship, accepting is the easy and obvious path. Refusing requires both courage and conviction. Fu Gu not only declined their overtures — he declared them "men of corrupt character." This was a bold and dangerous statement in an era when their faction held power.

💡 The Strategy

Fu Gu analyzed each man's fatal flaw: Xiahou Xuan had "great ambitions but a worried mind, capable only of generating hollow fame." He Yan and Deng Yang were "active but restless, learned but lacking focus, outwardly greedy and inwardly without moral compass." His conclusion was stark: "Keeping distance from them is not enough to avoid disaster — how much worse would it be to draw close?"

🏛 The Outcome

When Sima Yi launched the Gaopingling coup, all three men were executed along with their entire clans. Fu Gu, who had kept his distance, survived unscathed and went on to have a successful career. His prediction proved devastatingly accurate.

💛 Why This Matters

Fu Gu's solitude was a form of wisdom. While everyone else was dazzled by fame and talent, he saw the danger beneath. The courage to stand apart from the crowd — to say "these celebrated men are not what they seem" — requires a rare combination of insight and independence. It is the embodiment of "while all others are drunk, I alone remain sober."

🔮 The Deeper Lesson

The deeper lesson is about the halo effect: we tend to assume that famous, talented people must also be good, wise, and safe to be around. Fu Gu saw past this illusion. He recognized that charisma can mask character flaws, and that proximity to powerful but reckless people is not an asset but a liability. In modern social and professional life, the same principle applies: associating with people who have obvious character defects is risky, no matter how successful they appear to be today.

Reflection

1 When multiple people are fighting over something that involves you, do you prefer that someone else decides for you, or that you get to choose? Why?
2 How often do we assume we know what's best for someone else without simply asking them? Can you think of a time when asking directly would have been the better approach?
3 What makes 'asking the person involved' such a powerful — yet so often overlooked — solution? Why do people prefer to argue on someone else's behalf?
