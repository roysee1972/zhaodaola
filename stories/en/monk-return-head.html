LEVEL 07 · MONEY & WEALTH

The Night-Caller Case

"Monk, Return My Head!"

Life Principle
Greed is the root of all evil — both the merchant and the husband paid the price for their desires. Psychological defenses matter more than physical ones; inner fear will eventually expose the truth. Bystanders' observations are often more accurate than the parties' own judgments.

⏳ The Setting

During the Song Dynasty, a wealthy merchant desired a young married woman and offered her husband a large sum of money. The husband, blinded by greed, forced his wife to comply, arranging a nighttime dinner where she would 'entertain' the merchant. But when the merchant arrived late that night, he found the woman murdered — and her head missing. The case seemed unsolvable until an elderly witness came forward with a critical observation: the night-calling monk who used to pass through the neighborhood had vanished after the murder.

⚖ The Dilemma

The woman was dead, her head was gone, and the only clue was the sudden disappearance of a monk whose job was to chant through the streets at night. There was no body to examine (without the head), no witnesses to the crime, and no obvious motive beyond the sordid financial arrangement. How could the murderer be identified and forced to reveal where the head was hidden?

💡 The Strategy

The investigator tracked the monk to another district and devised a plan rooted in psychology. He had someone disguise their voice and call out from the woods at night, mimicking the dead woman's voice: 'Monk... return my head...' The monk, already haunted by guilt, heard the ghostly voice and panicked. In his terror, he blurted out the location of the head: 'On the rack at the Three Families' shop!' When the head was recovered and examined, it was found to be from a completely different person — revealing that the monk had murdered someone else ten years earlier and hidden that victim's head in the same manner. The monk was ultimately convicted of both murders.

🏛 The Outcome

The psychological trap worked perfectly. The monk's guilty conscience, prodded by the simulated ghostly voice, caused him to reveal the location of the hidden head. Further investigation uncovered a second murder committed a decade earlier. The monk was executed for both crimes.

💛 Why This Matters

This case is one of the most dramatic in Chinese legal history. The image of a disembodied voice calling 'Monk, return my head!' in a dark forest is haunting — and the monk's terrified response is both chilling and satisfying. It speaks to a universal truth: guilt is a prisoner that never sleeps. No matter how far you run or how well you hide, the weight of what you've done follows you. The monk could flee to another district, but he could not flee from himself.

🔮 The Deeper Lesson

The deeper principle is that 'fear drives truthful responses.' In moments of extreme psychological pressure, the rational mind loses control, and the instinct for self-preservation forces out the truth. This is the foundation of modern interrogation psychology — not physical force, but the strategic application of psychological pressure. The case also illustrates 'cross-temporal linking': the investigator connected a ten-year-old cold case to the current one through a single piece of evidence — the hidden head's origin. The broader lesson: the past never fully disappears. Old crimes leave traces that resurface when new events bring them to light.

Reflection

1 The monk was ultimately undone by his own fear. What unaddressed fears in your own life might be silently influencing your decisions and behavior?
2 An elderly observer's noticing the monk's absence was the key breakthrough. How well do you pay attention to what's 'missing' or 'absent' in situations you encounter?
3 The psychological trap — calling out 'Monk, return my head!' — exploited the monk's guilt. Is there a difference between psychological tactics used in investigation and manipulation? Where is the line?
