LEVEL 01 · FAMILY OF ORIGIN

He Wu (何武) & Zhang Yong (张咏)

Reading the Hidden Love in a Father's Will

Life Principle
Do not be bound by the surface meaning of words — seek to understand the true intention behind them. Protecting the vulnerable is the core value of justice. A father's love sometimes requires wisdom to decode; what appears heartless may contain the deepest care.

⏳ The Setting

In ancient China, two separate cases of inheritance disputes came before two different magistrates — He Wu (何武) and Zhang Yong (张咏). In each case, a wealthy father had written a will that appeared to disinherit his own young son in favor of a daughter or son-in-law. On the surface, the wills seemed clear, even cruel. But both magistrates sensed that something was deeply wrong with the apparent meaning.

⚖ The Dilemma

In He Wu's case, a wealthy man with a fortune of over 200,000 cash left everything to his daughter, leaving his three-year-old son nothing but a sword, to be delivered when the boy turned fifteen. In Zhang Yong's case, a wealthy man's will divided his estate: 30% to his son, 70% to his son-in-law. Both wills appeared to favor the daughter's family over the son. But both magistrates asked the same piercing question: what kind of father disinherits his own child?

💡 The Strategy

Both magistrates applied the same interpretive principle: when a document's content violates all常识 (common sense), read it in reverse. He Wu concluded that the father had given his estate to his daughter not out of love for her, but because he feared that if he left it to his young son, the daughter and her husband would harm the boy to claim the inheritance. The sword was the true inheritance token — a secret signal to be decoded by a wise official. Zhang Yong reached the same conclusion about the 30/70 split: the father deliberately inflated the son-in-law's share to protect his son. If the son appeared to be the favored heir, the son-in-law might have harmed him. By appearing to favor the son-in-law, the father ensured the boy's safety.

🏛 The Outcome

Both magistrates reversed the apparent terms of the wills. He Wu awarded the entire estate to the son, declaring that the daughter had merely been a custodian. Zhang Yong awarded 70% to the son and 30% to the son-in-law — exactly the reverse of what the will stated. In both cases, the father's true intention was fulfilled, and the vulnerable young sons received the protection and inheritance their fathers had sacrificed so much to ensure.

💛 Why This Matters

The emotional weight of these stories is immense. Imagine a father on his deathbed, knowing he cannot protect his child after he is gone. He cannot openly favor his son, because doing so would make the boy a target. So he writes a will that appears to disinherit the very child he loves most — trusting that someday, a wise reader will see through the deception. The love encoded in these documents is almost unbearable in its sacrifice and cunning. He Wu and Zhang Yong did not just interpret legal documents; they heard a dead father's desperate plea across the silence of the grave.

🔮 The Deeper Lesson

The underlying logic is that extraordinary situations demand extraordinary interpretations. When a father's will clearly violates all reasonable expectations — when it says something no loving father would say — the interpreter must look for the hidden intention. This principle has deep roots in Chinese legal philosophy and continues in modern jurisprudence: the concept of "legislative intent" in statutory interpretation, and "purposive construction" in contract law, both follow the same logic. Words are tools; meaning is the purpose they serve. When the tools and the purpose appear to conflict, trust the purpose.

2 Both fathers sacrificed their public image as loving parents in order to protect their children. Can you think of a situation where appearing to do the "wrong" thing was actually the most protective choice?
