Game of Life Level 1: Family of Origin
Why Do Parents' "It's for Your Own Good" Become the Deepest Wound?

Zhaodaola · The Twelve Levels of the Game of Life

The relationship with your parents: you can't win, and you can't run. If that resonates, congratulations — you've hit a roadblock at the very first level of the game of life.

Zhaodaola (zhaodaola.top) has designed twelve levels for the Game of Life. Seeking knowledge, making a living, marriage, midlife crisis… none of them are easy. But the hardest of all is Level One — Family of Origin.

Why?

Because with every other level, you at least have some choice. What major to study, what job to take, whom to marry — difficult as they are, at least you chose them yourself. The bitter fruit you planted with your own hands, you swallow it with tears and all.

Only with your family of origin, you had no choice at all.

You can't choose who your parents are. You can't choose what kind of family you're born into. You can't choose how your childhood unfolds. By the time you develop independent awareness, you discover you've already been "programmed" for the most part — personality, habits, thought patterns, emotional responses — so much of it has been carved into your bones.

That's the cruelty of family of origin: it shapes you the most, yet you had the least say in it.

Inside the door lies the past that was scripted for you; outside it, the direction you can choose

Where the root of the problem lies

🔒
Control and Boundaries

Parents are too controlling — what you eat, what you wear, who your friends are, who you date, everything. You want to push back, and they say "It's all for your own good." You try to set boundaries, and they say "You've grown wings — you're so ungrateful."

You're caught in a double bind: comply, and you suffocate; resist, and you carry guilt.

💔
Favoritism and Neglect

When there are several children, the parents clearly favor one over the others. The favored one grows bold and entitled; the overlooked one desperately tries to please. In adulthood, the neglected child often lives exhausted — deep down feeling they're never good enough, constantly needing to prove they deserve to be loved.

This kind of wound is invisible on the surface, but it keeps bleeding.

⚖️
Sons Over Daughters

In some families, daughters are "outsiders" from birth. Resources go to the son; responsibilities go to the daughter. Parents pay for the son's apartment, but when the daughter marries, they collect bride price to "compensate" the family.

The daughters are left wondering: Am I part of this family, or am I an outsider?

📢
Marriage Pressure and Guilt-Tripping

Every holiday, relatives take turns: "Do you have a partner yet?" "How old are you and still not married?" "Look at so-and-so — their kid is already in kindergarten." Parents are even more anxious: "If you don't get married, we can't hold our heads up in front of the relatives."

You desperately want to say: Why should my life revolve around your reputation? But you can't get the words out.

Why is this level so hard?

Other levels are hard, but at least the "enemy" is clear — work pressure, financial hardship, workplace competition. The difficulty of family of origin lies in the fact that the line between "friend and foe" is blurred.

Difficulty #1: The Shackles of Blood

No matter how much your parents hurt you, they are still your parents. You can fall out with your boss, cut ties with a friend, but you can't "sever the relationship" with your parents. Society won't allow it. Morality won't allow it. Your own heart won't allow it.

So many people choose to endure. Endure until they're internally wounded. Endure until they fall into depression. Endure until they themselves become the ones inflicting harm.

Difficulty #2: Moral Blackmail

Chinese culture places enormous weight on "filial piety." No matter what your parents did, the moment you show even a hint of dissatisfaction, you're branded as "unfilial."

"Parents are never wrong." "No matter how bad they are, they raised you." "Look at other people's children — so filial." These words are like a tightening curse, leaving you unable to voice your pain.

Difficulty #3: The Impossibility of Change

You can't change your parents. You tried communicating — they didn't listen. You tried pushing back — they doubled down. You tried pleasing them — they took it for granted. Decades of ingrained thought patterns and habits can't be undone by a few words from you.

Difficulty #4: Guilt and Entanglement

What torments you most is this: you hate them, but you also love them. You want to escape, but you worry about them. You want to cut ties, but in the quiet of night you wonder: what will happen when they grow old?

This tangled love and hate — that is the deepest pain of family of origin.

Common traps we fall into

❌
Trap #1: Trying to Change Your Parents

"I'll make them see their mistakes." "I'll make them apologize." "I'll make them understand me."

Result: communication turns into arguments, arguments into cold wars, cold wars into deeper wounds.

Truth: you can't change people shaped by decades. You can only change yourself.

❌
Trap #2: Using Distance as the Solution

"I'll move far away — out of sight, out of mind." Result: physical distance is achieved, but the psychological entanglement remains. You still fall apart when they call. You still think about childhood wounds in the middle of the night.

Truth: distance is a necessary step, but it's not the destination. True freedom lives in your heart, not in geography.

❌
Trap #3: People-Pleasing for Approval

"If I do better, they'll love me." "If I get into a good school, land a good job, marry well — they'll be proud of me." Result: you succeed, but they're still not satisfied. Or they are satisfied, but you've burned yourself out.

Truth: you don't need to be "good enough" to earn love. You are worthy of love as you are.

❌
Trap #4: Using Family of Origin as an Excuse

"The reason I am this way is all my family's fault." "Where I am today is because of what my parents did." Result: you trap yourself in the role of "victim" and can never move forward.

Truth: your family of origin is your starting point, not your endpoint. You can choose not to let the trauma continue.

Finding the roadblock, finding a way through

How do you get past the family of origin level? There's no standard answer, because every family is different. But here are some approaches that might help.

💡
Approach #1: From "Changing Them" to "Understanding Yourself"

Stop trying to change your parents. Turn your attention back to yourself. Ask: Why do I fall apart over a single sentence from my parents? Why can't I stop trying to please everyone? Why do I both crave and fear intimacy?

When you understand yourself, your parents' words and actions will no longer trigger you so easily. This isn't surrender — it's growing armor.

💡
Approach #2: Set Boundaries, Not Cut Ties

Boundaries don't mean "You're dead to me." They mean "I love you, but there are things I won't accept." Boundaries are: you can care about me, but you can't make my decisions. You can give me advice, but you can't demand that I follow it. You can live near me, but you can't live in my house.

Setting boundaries is hard. You'll go through a painful "battle phase." But if you hold firm, your parents will eventually accept a new way of relating.

💡
Approach #3: Give Yourself Permission to Be "Unfilial"

The poison of Chinese-style filial piety is this: it makes you feel that any disobedience to your parents equals being unfilial. But true filial piety isn't unconditional obedience — it's mutual respect.

You can refuse your parents' unreasonable demands and still love them. You can live life on your own terms and still care about them. Giving yourself permission to be "unfilial" is the only way to be truly filial.

💡
Approach #4: Don't Let Trauma Be "Inherited"

If you already have children, the most important thing you can do is: become aware. Notice whether you're repeating your parents' patterns — you hated their controlling nature, but are you controlling your own child? You hated their coldness, but do you rarely express love to your child?

Awareness is the first step to change. The moment you realize you're repeating the cycle, you gain the power to choose differently.

💡
Approach #5: Make Peace with What Can't Be Changed

Some wounds will never fully heal. Some regrets can never be made whole. But that doesn't mean you can't live well.

Making peace with your family of origin isn't about forgiving your parents' wrongs — it's about no longer letting those wrongs define your life. You can carry your scars forward. Scars are proof that you survived, not a reason you can't keep going.

You can't choose your starting point, but you can choose your direction.
You can't rewrite the past, but you can redefine the future.

To everyone who was hurt in their family of origin, I want to say:

Your pain is real. Those wounds really happened. You don't have to pretend you're fine, and you don't have to force yourself to forgive.

But you deserve to move beyond that pain. You deserve a life of your own.

Not to prove anything to anyone, but for yourself.
