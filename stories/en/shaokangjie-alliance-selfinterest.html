LEVEL 08 · SOCIAL MAZE

Shao Yong (邵雍)

Alliances Built on Self-Interest Will Become Rivalries

Life Principle
See through the nature of alliances. Never be deceived by surface harmony. Partnerships based on shared values endure; partnerships based on shared interests are fragile.

⏳ The Setting

During the Northern Song Dynasty, Wang Anshi's reforms split the court into fierce factions. After Wang Anshi was dismissed from office, Lü Huiqing took over as Vice Commissioner and continued implementing the New Policies. Shao Yong, a philosopher watching from the sidelines, had a unique perspective on the unfolding drama.

⚖ The Dilemma

Wang Anshi and Lü Huiqing appeared to be close allies united by a common cause. But Shao Yong saw something others missed: their partnership was built on shared advantage, not shared conviction. Such alliances, he knew, were inherently unstable.

💡 The Strategy

Shao Yong declared plainly: "Wang Anshi and Lü Huiqing originally came together through self-interest. Now that their interests compete, they will naturally become enemies." He also showed his own caution in practice — declining dinner invitations to avoid any association with people who might bring trouble. His ability to read others was matched by his discipline in managing himself.

🏛 The Outcome

Lü Huiqing eventually turned against Wang Anshi, even exposing his private letters to undermine him. Wang Anshi was forced out of office a second time, and the reform movement suffered a devastating blow. Shao Yong's prediction was vindicated in the most painful way possible.

💛 Why This Matters

Shao Yong's detachment was itself a form of wisdom. While others were swept up in the passion of reform or the fury of opposition, he maintained the clarity to see human nature at work beneath the political surface. His refusal to attend certain dinners — "such caution in the space of a single meal" — showed that his insight was not just theoretical but lived.

🔮 The Deeper Lesson

The deeper logic is about the foundations of partnership. When two people unite because they share the same enemy or the same opportunity, the partnership holds only as long as the external pressure persists. Once that pressure eases, the internal competition surfaces. In modern business and politics, this pattern repeats endlessly: mergers of convenience become hostile takeovers, political alliances become bitter rivalries. The test is always the same: do these people share values, or only interests?
