LEVEL 03 · ENTERING THE WORLD

甘露寺僧

—黄泥验金样

Life Principle
A confession does not equal the truth — testimony must be verified. Lies can fabricate a story, but cannot fabricate the details of lived experience. Isolated blind testing is an effective method for verifying the consistency of group testimony. A prudent attitude matters more than rushing to close a case.

⏳ The Setting

Monks at Ganlu Temple (甘露寺) accused the former head monk of squandering the temple's communal gold during the transfer of temple property. After the trial, the former head monk had confessed and accepted his punishment, and the trail of the stolen funds seemed clear. But the presiding official noticed that although the man had confessed, no one had actually determined where the money was specifically spent — "the place of expenditure was never fully investigated."

⚖ The Dilemma

The former head monk had confessed, but the whereabouts of the stolen funds remained unknown — this itself was suspicious. If the money had truly been squandered, there should be specific spending records; if none existed, it might be a false accusation or something more sinister. How to verify whether these monks had ever actually handled the gold became the key to uncovering the truth.

🎯 Motives

The official's goal was to trace the true whereabouts of the stolen funds and determine whether the former head monk had actually squandered the communal gold. The accusing monks' motive might have been to frame the former head monk in order to seize temple authority, or the former head monk might indeed have squandered the gold.

🧩 The Framework

The official adopted a "blind test verification" cognitive framework. He reasoned: if these monks had truly handled the communal gold, they would know its shape and form; if they had not, they could not describe its specific appearance. By having monks describe the gold in isolation, he could verify whether they were lying.

🧠 The Mindset

The official was meticulous in his thinking, refusing to close the case hastily simply because there was "already a confession." He recognized that "confession" did not equal "truth" and that further verification was needed. The monks, meanwhile, believed their confession would suffice to close the matter, never imagining the official would employ such a clever method to verify their testimony.

💡 The Strategy

The official summoned several sedan chairs and ordered the monks involved to enter them separately, with the chair openings facing the wall so they could not see each other. He then instructed each monk to mold yellow clay into the shape of the gold they had supposedly handled, as evidence. Not one monk could mold the correct shape of the gold — because none of them had ever actually handled it. Faced with this irrefutable proof, the monks admitted to making a false accusation.

🔬 The Mental Model

The core model is "isolated blind testing" — isolating suspects and asking each to describe the same object; if descriptions are consistent, they may be true; if inconsistent or impossible to produce, they are fabricated. Another model is "experiential knowledge verification" — someone who has truly handled an object can describe its characteristics; someone who has not cannot.

🏛 The Outcome

Through the yellow clay blind test, the monks could not mold the correct shape of the gold and admitted to the false accusation. The former head monk's wrongful conviction was overturned, and the false accusers were punished. This case demonstrated the unique value of "isolated blind testing" in verifying the truthfulness of testimony.

💛 Why This Matters

An innocent monk falsely accused by his fellow monks, facing charges of embezzling temple property — the pain of betrayal by those you trusted is chilling. The official did not close the case hastily, but used a clever method to verify the truth, saving an innocent person's reputation and fate. The truth is worth waiting for; justice is worth pursuing.

🔮 The Deeper Lesson

The underlying logic is "lived experience cannot be fabricated" — someone who has truly experienced something can describe its details; someone who has not can only construct a vague framework. Extending to the modern world: police "cognitive interviews" require suspects to describe scene details to verify actual presence; intellectual property authentication requires "recreating the creative process" to verify originality.
