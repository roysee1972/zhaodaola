LEVEL 09 · INTERESTS & PASSIONS

Zong Zongmín (宗威愍)

Using Wine and Women to Detain a Guest

Life Principle
A head-on confrontation is not always the best option. Finding where the other person's desires lie and using the satisfaction of those desires to achieve your goals often yields twice the result with half the effort.

⏳ The Setting

During the Northern Song dynasty, Zong Rulin (宗泽) served as magistrate of Wendeng County. Huang Rong, a fellow jinshi graduate serving as an instructor in Qingzhou, was demoted after submitting a memorial to the throne. While passing through Wendeng, he fell ill with a cold and could not continue his journey, but the military escort officer pressed him relentlessly, refusing to allow any stop.

⚖ The Dilemma

Huang Rong was too ill to travel, yet the escort officer insisted on pushing forward without compromise. Zong Rulin, as the local magistrate, wanted to help, but directly asking the officer to relent would be futile — the man was set in his ways and impossible to persuade.

💡 The Strategy

Zong Rulin first arranged for Huang Rong to stay at the official guesthouse and sent physicians to treat him. Simultaneously, he secretly arranged for local clerks to take turns inviting the escort officer out for drinking and entertainment, frequenting the military camp's pleasure quarters. The officer, addicted to wine and women, became so absorbed that he refused to leave. Only when Huang Rong had fully recovered was the officer finally prodded back onto the road.

🏛 The Outcome

Huang Rong received full rest and recovered completely. The escort officer remained entirely oblivious to having been manipulated. Zong Rulin completed his rescue without raising the slightest suspicion.

💛 Why This Matters

Stepping forward to help a friend in distress without resorting to brute force, instead using wisdom to resolve the predicament — this exemplifies the ancient way of valuing loyalty while employing strategy.

🔮 The Deeper Lesson

The root of execution is willpower, and willpower is most easily eroded by desire. Rather than confronting someone's will, it is more effective to satisfy their desires and thereby dismantle their resolve. In modern management, the practice of "using benefits to dissolve discontent" follows a similar logic.

Reflection

1 Zong Zongmín used the enemy's own desires against him rather than confronting him directly. Can you think of a situation in your life where satisfying someone's ego or appetite might have been more effective than arguing with them?
2 The guard had no idea he was being manipulated — he thought the hospitality was genuine. How can you tell the difference between sincere generosity and strategic generosity in your own relationships?
3 Is there a time when you needed to delay someone's action without them knowing? What does this story teach about the ethics and effectiveness of indirect influence?
