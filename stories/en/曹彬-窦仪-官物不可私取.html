LEVEL 06 · MIDLIFE

Cao Bin, Dou Yi

— Official Property Must Not Be Taken for Private Use

Life Principle
Drawing a clear line between public and private is the professional bottom line. Using personal resources to cover official inconveniences both follows the rules and preserves goodwill.

⏳ The Setting

In the final years of the Five Dynasties, Emperor Shizong of the Later Zhou reigned while Zhao Kuangyin was still a subject. The political situation was volatile, the relationship between ruler and ministers delicate, and a power transition between the old sovereign and the new rising star could occur at any moment.

⚖ The Dilemma

Cao Bin managed official wine, and Zhao Kuangyin requested some. Dou Yi was ordered to inventory the treasury, and Zhao Kuangyin sent a trusted aide to obtain silk. Both faced direct demands from the future emperor — refusing might offend a powerful figure, but complying would violate their duties.

🎯 Motives

Cao Bin's goal was to maintain his relationship with Zhao Kuangyin without violating his duties. Dou Yi's goal was to ensure the treasury accounts were clean, leaving a compliant record for the subsequent handover. Both chose to uphold institutional standards.

🧩 The Framework

Both established the cognitive framework of "separating official conduct from personal conduct" — official property belongs to the state and cannot be given away privately, but personal resources can be used to fulfill human courtesies, thus following rules without hurting feelings.

🧠 The Mindset

Cao Bin remained composed, neither sycophantic nor rigid, finding a way to satisfy both sides by buying his own wine. Dou Yi was more forthright — knowing the other party might one day become emperor, he still insisted on the principle that "once registered, it becomes official property."

💡 The Strategy

Cao Bin adopted a "personal funds fill public gaps" strategy — refusing official wine but treating guests out of his own pocket. Dou Yi adopted a "procedural compliance" strategy — emphasizing that once items were registered, formal procedures must be followed. Both strategies held the bottom line, but in different ways.

🔬 The Mental Model

The core mental model is "institutions supersede personal relationships." In an era of uncertain power, upholding institutional standards is the best way to protect yourself — even if it means offending a future emperor, you earn the reputation of "not deceiving one's master."

🏛 The Outcome

After Zhao Kuangyin ascended the throne, Cao Bin became a trusted general, and Dou Yi was also given important responsibilities. The trust both earned through following rules far exceeded what any short-term favor could have brought.

💛 Why This Matters

The phrase "bought his own wine to treat him" is deeply human — refusing the public resource but paying out of pocket to entertain, both upholding principle and giving face. It is a model of workplace wisdom.

🔮 The Deeper Lesson

The underlying logic is "institutional credibility is a long-term asset." In the modern workplace, when financial staff or auditors face leaders' "flexible demands," this case remains profoundly relevant.
